8. NOVEMBER 1923 – Beer Hall Putsch

On the night of November 8-9, 1923, Adolf Hitler attempted to launch a revolution from the Munich beer hall Bürgerbräukeller that was meant to overthrow the Weimar system and establish a national socialist order. The putsch was prepared in collaboration with General Ludendorff and Bavarian right-wing politicians, but its preparation was chaotic and relied on circumstances that failed to materialize. Police and military units loyal to the lawful government quickly suppressed the putsch; sixteen Nazis were killed in a shootout at Feldherrnhalle, and Hitler was arrested and tried.

The Trial as a Propaganda Platform

The trial of Hitler and his co-defendants took place in February and March 1924. The court gave him an opportunity that he fully exploited: the interrogations and closing speech became a propaganda platform where Hitler formulated his political program before a German-wide audience. Instead of being convicted of high treason with the severity the law permitted, he was sentenced to merely five years, of which he served only thirteen months. In Landsberg Prison, he dictated the first volume of Mein Kampf to Rudolf Hess. The leniency of the Bavarian court toward right-wing extremism is itself a telling document of the selective administration of justice in 1920s Germany.

The Putsch as an Unheeded Warning

The Beer Hall Putsch failed as a military enterprise, but paradoxically succeeded as a political project. Hitler gained national publicity, the NSDAP entered the public consciousness, and the legend of Hitler as a martyr jailed for his ideals began to be cultivated. Democracy defended itself in 1923, but it did not adequately grasp the signal the putsch sent: that an organized force willing to use violence to overthrow the democratic state existed in Germany, and that parts of the judiciary and army sympathized with it at the least. This inconsistency would return to haunt the Weimar Republic with full force a decade later.
