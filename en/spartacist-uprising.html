5. JANUARY 1919 – Spartacist Uprising

The first fifteen days of 1919 determined the shape of the German revolution for years to come. The Spartacist League—the seed of the future Communist Party of Germany (KPD)—together with revolutionary trade union committees sought to overthrow the government of Friedrich Ebert and the social democrats and establish a Soviet-type government in Berlin. The uprising, known as the Spartacist or January uprising, was suppressed at the cost of bloody street fighting, in which hundreds of people on both sides lost their lives.

Freikorps: The Right-Wing Fists of the Republic

Ebert and Defense Minister Gustav Noske relied on voluntary corps—the Freikorps—in suppressing the uprising. These units were made up mainly of front-line veterans who found no place in civilian life and for whom violence was a language they knew well. Their political orientation was distinctly right-wing and nationalist, and their approach to enemies brutal. It was Freikorps members who on January 15, 1919, arrested, tortured, and murdered Rosa Luxemburg and Karl Liebknecht—the two most prominent figures of the radical left. Their bodies were thrown into a canal, where Luxemburg's remains were found only after months. This murder became a symbol of the brutality with which the nascent republic dealt with its internal opponents.

Loss of Freedom for the Radical Left

For communists and the revolutionary left, the suppression of the Spartacist uprising meant a fundamental defeat and lasting persecution. Key leaders were dead or fled, organizational networks were broken, and public activity was severely hampered. The republic that proclaimed freedom of association and speech proved these freedoms had limits—and these limits were enforced by force of arms. The paradox was deep: the social democratic government, which came from the same labor movement as the Spartacists, used right-wing paramilitary units to eliminate left-wing opposition. This choice split the German left in a way that was never healed and severely weakened democratic forces at the time when the Weimar Republic needed them most.

Fragile Freedom and Its Price

January 1919 showed how fragile political rights acquired only two months before could be. Freedom of speech, association, and political organization—values loudly proclaimed in the turbulent November 1918—received their first serious test and proved reliable only for those who stood within the limits acceptable to the ruling coalition. Those who stood outside soon discovered that freedom without strong institutional protection could be revoked overnight by force.
