9. NOVEMBER 1918 – Proclamation of the Republic

On the morning of November 9, 1918, Kaiser Wilhelm II abdicated and departed into exile in the Netherlands. At a moment when it was still unclear what the republic would even look like, socialist Philipp Scheidemann, from a window of the Reichstag building in Berlin, anticipated events and proclaimed the German Republic. A few hours later, Karl Liebknecht proclaimed a free socialist republic from Berlin Palace. These two proclamations on the same day symbolized a deep split in the revolutionary movement, but both voices agreed on one thing: the forty-year-old Hohenzollern monarchy had ended.

What the Fall of the Monarchy Meant for Freedom

For millions of German citizens, November 9, 1918, was the day when the condition in which they had lived for four years came to an end. Wartime censorship ceased to apply, the military dictatorship of Hindenburg and Ludendorff lost its legitimacy, and the streets filled with demonstrators who could for the first time since the summer of 1914 say aloud what they thought. Political parties could again act freely. The press cautiously, but rapidly, began to get used to the fact that it did not have to wait for the censor's stamp. In the course of mere days, things emerged that a week before had been impossible: open debates about the future of the state, the establishment of revolutionary councils, public statements by opponents of the existing order.

The Republic as a New Beginning and an Open Question

The proclamation of the republic was simultaneously an opening of Pandora's box of questions for which there were no ready answers. Would the new state be a parliamentary democracy after the model of France and Britain, or a radical soviet republic after the model of Russia? Who would govern—the social democratic majority or the revolutionary left? What would be the role of the army, industrialists, and the old bureaucracy? These questions were not merely academic—they determined whether the freedom proclaimed on November 9 would have solid institutional backing, or would remain merely a proclamation on the pavement of turbulent days. The Weimar Republic, which emerged from this chaos, bore from its first day the marks of these unanswered questions.

The End of an Era

The fall of the monarchy represented a qualitative turning point for Germany. For the first time in modern history, Germans could build a state whose legitimacy did not derive from dynastic tradition and divine will, but from the will of the people. This opportunity, though it came amidst dramatic and chaotic circumstances, was truly unprecedented in the history of German freedom.
