JULY 25, 1914 – DECLARATION OF MARTIAL LAW AFTER THE ASSASSINATION OF THE HEIR TO THE THRONE, RESTRICTION OF CIVIL LIBERTIES

In response to the assassination of the heir to the Austrian throne, Archduke Franz Ferdinand, on June 28, 1914, Austria-Hungary underwent a fundamental restriction of civil freedoms on July 25 of the same year. The empire prepared to declare martial law and formally did so two days later. This measure was intended to help prevent unrest, nationalist activities, and possible sabotage at a time of rising international tensions.

Restrictions on Freedom of Speech and Press

Authorities gained the power to restrict freedom of assembly, press, and speech. Newspapers were subject to strict censorship and public gatherings were often banned. The state sought to prevent the dissemination of information or opinions that could destabilize the political situation or incite resistance against the government. Police and the military gained broader authority to act against persons suspected of anti-government activity, and postal secrecy was suspended. Another consequence of preparing martial law was the strengthening of military authority: military courts could in some cases decide on civil matters.

Freedom as the Price of War Preparation

This restriction of freedoms on the eve of World War I showed how dramatically Europe's political situation had escalated after the Sarajevo assassination and how quickly states began preparing for a major armed conflict, regardless of the freedoms of their citizens.
