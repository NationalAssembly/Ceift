July 25, 1914 – Declaration of Martial Law

Following the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand, heir to the throne, in Sarajevo on June 28, 1914, Austria-Hungary launched a series of steps that led to a fundamental restriction of civil liberties. Already during the July Crisis, the Imperial Council was suspended and the country began to be governed by emergency decrees. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assassination_of_Archduke_Franz_Ferdinand [Wikipedia – Assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand]

Suspension of Parliament

The Imperial Council had effectively been put out of action even before the outbreak of war – Prime Minister Count Stürgkh had adjourned it as early as March 1914, and it did not reconvene until late May 1917. Austria thus entered the war with a non-functioning parliament, with the emperor declaring war without any parliamentary oversight. For Czech political life, this meant the loss of a key forum in which Czech deputies could advance national interests. https://www.psp.cz/sqw/hp.sqw?k=697 [Parliament of the Czech Republic – History of Parliamentarism]

Restriction of Civil Liberties

After the declaration of the state of war, constitutional civil rights were abolished: freedom of speech, assembly, and association, as well as the inviolability of postal correspondence. In September 1914, parliamentary immunity was also revoked. Partial mobilization was declared on July 26, 1914, and general mobilization on July 31. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austria-Hungary [Wikipedia – Austria-Hungary]

In the Czech lands, this meant in practice bans on cultural and sporting events, censorship of books, and prohibition of newspapers and magazines. Anti-Austrian statements were punishable by death – between 1914 and 1916, 24 death sentences were handed down. The publication of anti-Austrian manifestos was prosecuted with extreme severity. https://www.dejepis.com/ucebnice/ceske-zeme-na-pocatku-prvni-svetove-valky/ [Dejepis.com – Czech Lands at the Outbreak of the First World War]

War as the End of Democratization

For Czech politics and society, the onset of the wartime regime represented a profound turning point. Everything that had previously been considered steps towards the democratization of Austria – above all the struggle for universal suffrage – lost its meaning with the first shots of the war. The war signified the triumph of absolutism and the exclusion of popular representation from political decision-making. https://masarykovaakademie.cz/wp-content/uploads/V%C5%A1eobecn%C3%A9_hlas_pr%C3%A1vo_online_final.pdf [Masarykova Academy – Universal Suffrage]

The Road to War

The Austro-Hungarian government used the assassination as a pretext to declare war on Serbia on July 28, 1914, effectively triggering the First World War. Serbia accepted almost all conditions of the Austrian ultimatum, refusing only those that directly infringed on its state sovereignty. Nevertheless, Vienna chose war – and with it, the permanent suppression of democratic life throughout the entire monarchy. https://www.moderni-dejiny.cz/clanek/rakousko-uherske-ultimatum-srbsku-23-7-1914/ [Moderní-Dějiny.cz – Austro-Hungarian Ultimatum to Serbia]
