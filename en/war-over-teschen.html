JANUARY 23, 1919 – THE SEVEN-DAY WAR OVER TESCHEN

On Thursday, January 23, 1919, at 1 p.m., Czechoslovak forces under the command of Major Josef Šnejdárek launched a military operation that historians have since called the Seven-Day War. The opponent was not Hungary or remnants of Habsburg structures, but Poland – a state that, like Czechoslovakia, was barely three months old and found itself on the opposite side of the same territorial dispute. Teschen Silesia, an industrially important and ethnically diverse region on the Moravian-Silesian-Polish border, became the arena of a conflict that would leave marks on Czech-Polish relations for decades to come.

Who Has the Right to Teschen?

Teschen Silesia was economically invaluable: it contained rich coal mines in the Karviná region, mills in Třinec, and – most importantly – the Košice-Bohumin railway, the only railroad connection between Czech lands and eastern Slovakia. Without control over this line, the republic could not effectively govern Slovak territory. Yet the region's population was ethnically so mixed that no simple ethnic solution existed. The 1910 census recorded 54.8% Polish speakers, 27.1% Czech speakers, and 18.1% German speakers – though the Polish category included the so-called Šlonzaks (Silesians), whose political sympathies differed from Polish nationalists and many of whom preferred Czechoslovakia. Both sides invoked different arguments: Czechs claimed historical rights to Silesian heritage, Poles relied on linguistic and ethnic principles.

Polish Elections as Pretext

The immediate cause of the Czechoslovak attack was Poland's decision to hold elections to the Sejm at the end of January 1919, with voting to take place even in the part of Teschen controlled by Poles – despite a provisional agreement the two sides had concluded after the war. Prague saw this as a unilateral attempt to fix Polish claims to the region through an electoral act. At a joint meeting of the government and President Masaryk on January 17, 1919, the decision was made: militarily occupy all disputed territory and drive Polish forces across the Biala River. The Ministry of National Defense issued the order on January 19, and the operation began four days later.

The Course: Seven Days of Advance

Šnejdárek's forces, composed primarily of legionnaires returning from France and Italy, advanced rapidly. On the first day alone, Bohumin, Petrovice, and Šumbark fell. On January 24, Šnejdárek took Karviná and Frýštat. On January 27, Teschen surrendered without a fight, and Třinec fell the same day. Polish forces under General Franciszek Latinik retreated eastward – their situation was complicated by the fact that Poland's main forces were engaged at the time in fighting Ukrainian troops over eastern Galicia. The Czechoslovaks had clear superiority: they had over four thousand troops compared to roughly five Polish battalions with artillery and volunteers. The Czechoslovak advance halted at Skoczów (Skočov) on January 30, where Šnejdárek planned a decisive assault. It never came.

Pressure from the Entente and Armistice

The Allied powers gathered at the Paris Peace Conference watched the war between two new allies with alarm. On January 31, 1919, they forced both sides to a ceasefire. That same day, Šnejdárek signed the armistice, and on February 26, 1919, Czechoslovak forces withdrew to a new demarcation line set by Paris. The line was less favorable to Prague than what the army had occupied – Poland retained control over part of Teschen including a strategic section of the railway. Final division came only on July 28, 1920, when following international arbitration, Czechoslovakia received roughly one-third of the disputed territory – the industrial Karviná, Bohumin, and Třinec – while Poland gained Teschen and its surroundings. The Košice-Bohumin railway remained under Czechoslovak administration.

The Price and Consequences for Freedom

The Seven-Day War cost the Czechoslovak side dozens of dead and hundreds of wounded; precise figures vary in sources. Militarily, it was a tactical success; diplomatically, a mixed result; and relationally, a lasting wound. Poland saw the entire episode as betrayal by a neighbor who chose attack over solidarity with a newly formed state. This mistrust poisoned Czech-Polish relations precisely when alliance between the two against Germany was vital – and it manifested fully in October 1938, when Poland seized Teschen without a shot during the Munich Agreement as part of its own expansionist policy. Territorial disputes, where one side views the outcome as injustice, are rarely settled by a single agreement; Teschen confirmed this with bitter precision.
