19. JANUARY 1919 – Elections Including Women

Less than three months after the fall of the monarchy and amid the chaos of a revolutionary transitional period, Germany dared to do something unthinkable just months earlier: hold the first truly democratic nationwide election in the country's history. The elections to the Weimar National Assembly, held on January 19, 1919, were not merely an administrative act – they were a demonstration that the republic meant what it said about democracy.

Breakthrough: Women's Suffrage

The most revolutionary aspect of these elections was the complete and unconditional introduction of women's voting rights. In the German Empire, women had no voice in politics – not in state parliaments or the Imperial Parliament. The November 1918 revolution changed that overnight. Women who were not permitted to vote as recently as summer 1918 could in January 1919 not only cast ballots but also run for office. And they did: 37 women were elected to the National Assembly out of a total of 423 deputies – approximately 8.7 percent. This figure was exceptional in the context of Europe at the time. Alongside Germany and the new Czechoslovakia, women's suffrage was exercised in only a few other states at that time – Finland, Norway, and Denmark.

Participation and Results

Voter participation exceeded 83 percent of eligible voters, a figure that showed Germans took their new political role seriously. The strongest party was the Social Democratic Party of Germany (SPD) with nearly 38 percent of votes, followed by the Center Party with more than 19 percent and the German Democratic Party (DDP) with approximately 18 percent. These three parties together formed the so-called Weimar coalition, which then drafted the constitution and gave the republic its democratic foundation. The Assembly's meeting in Weimar – a small Thuringian town of Goethe and Schiller – had special symbolic significance, held far from the revolutionary chaos of Berlin.

Historical Context of Free Election

The elections of January 19, 1919, were the direct opposite of everything the Kriegsgefahrenzustand (war emergency) of July 1914 had been. There was a state that suspended freedoms without debate; here was a state that for the first time extended political rights to the entire adult population without exception. This contrast was perfectly clear to Germans who had experienced both, and it was one of the reasons why the Weimar Republic – despite all its later crises – enjoyed genuine support from much of the population at the beginning of its existence.
