Universal and equal suffrage for men

extension of suffrage in the Czech lands in 1907 represented a significant political reform within Austria-Hungary and the granting of equal voting rights to a large part of the male population of what is now the Czech Republic.

Until then, the electoral system was based on so-called curiae, where groups such as the aristocracy, the church, or the bourgeoisie had greater influence. In 1907, however, Emperor Franz Joseph I. approved extensive electoral reforms that abolished this system. The empire was divided into 516 single-member constituencies, 194 of which were allocated to the Czech lands. Today, the single-member constituency system (better known by its modern abbreviation FPTP) is used in countries such as Canada and the United Kingdom. The 1907 reform also introduced universal, equal, secret, and direct suffrage for all men over the age of 24. This meant that, for the first time, workers, farmers, and other less wealthy groups of the population were able to vote directly on a large scale. Elections were held to the Imperial Council in Vienna, the equivalent of today's parliament, and all men over the age of 30 were eligible to be elected./p>

This change significantly increased political participation among the inhabitants of the Czech lands. The reform contributed to greater democratization of political life in the empire, with social democratic parties, for example, beginning to play an important role. However, Austria-Hungary remained a constitutional monarchy, where the emperor still wielded considerable power. Moreover, the government was not fully accountable to parliament.

The curiae, which functioned before the imperial reform of 1907, were an electoral system in which different groups of the population elected their representatives separately. These groups were allocated a number of seats in the Imperial Council in advance, so the votes of the population were not equal. The more numerous group of townspeople, who paid at least 10 gold coins in direct taxes annually, had 71 representatives in parliament, while the much less numerous landowners, with a census of 500 gold coins, elected 70 representatives. Only men who owned land and paid the minimum census in direct taxes were eligible to vote. The census was 10 gold coins, and from 1882 onwards only five gold coins. A fifth "general" curia was added in 1896, in which all men over the age of 24 could vote, regardless of the taxes they paid. However, unlike the other curiae, the rural and general curiae voted indirectly. The entire curia system was replaced by the 1907 reform with a system of single-member constituencies, where all men over the age of 24 had the right to vote, which was now equal, regardless of property.
