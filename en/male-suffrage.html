UNIVERSAL AND EQUAL MALE SUFFRAGE

The expansion of voting rights in the Czech lands in 1907 represented a significant political reform within Austria-Hungary and the acquisition of equal voting rights for a substantial part of the male population of what is now the Czech Republic.

The Curia System Before the Reform

Until then, the electoral system was based on the so-called curiae, where the aristocracy, the clergy, and the bourgeoisie had greater influence. The curiae that operated before the imperial reform of 1907 were an electoral system in which various groups of the population elected their representatives separately. Each group was assigned a number of seats in advance, so the vote of the population was not equal. The more numerous group of townspeople who paid a direct tax census of at least 10 guilders annually received 71 representatives in parliament, while the much smaller group of large landholders, with a census of 500 guilders, elected 70 deputies. Only men who owned land and paid a minimum census in direct taxes could vote. This was 10 guilders, and from 1882 only five guilders. A fifth "universal" curia was added in 1896, in which all men over 24 could vote regardless of taxes paid. However, unlike the other curiae, the rural and universal curiae voted indirectly. The entire curia system was replaced by the 1907 reform with a system of single-mandate constituencies, where all men over 24 had voting rights that were now equal, regardless of property.

The 1907 Reform

In 1907, however, Emperor Franz Joseph I approved a sweeping electoral reform that abolished this system. The empire was divided into 516 single-mandate constituencies, of which 194 went to the Czech lands. Nowadays, the single-mandate constituency system (better known by the modern acronym FPTP) is used for example in Canada and the United Kingdom. The 1907 reform also newly introduced universal, equal, secret, and direct voting rights for all men over 24. As a result, workers, farmers, and other less wealthy groups could directly vote for the first time in large numbers. Elections were held to the Imperial Council (Reichsrat) in Vienna, the equivalent of today's parliament, and all men over 30 could be elected to it.

Consequences for Political Life

This change significantly increased political participation among the population of the Czech lands. The reform contributed to greater democratization of political life in the empire; for example, social democratic parties began to play a significant role. Nevertheless, Austria-Hungary remained a constitutional monarchy in which the emperor still had considerable power. Moreover, the government was not fully accountable to parliament.
