30. AUGUST 1924 – The Dawes Plan

On August 30, 1924, the Dawes Plan was signed in London, named after American banker and later Vice President Charles Dawes. The plan responded to the crisis into which Germany had been plunged by a combination of reparation burden, Ruhr occupation, and hyperinflation. It provided new terms for paying war reparations, secured substantial American loans for Germany, and created a framework for stabilizing the mark and the economy as a whole.

Economic Recovery and the "Golden Twenties"

Thanks to a massive influx of American capital, the German economy recovered from the crisis remarkably quickly. Industry revived, unemployment fell, real wages rose, and Germany experienced in the second half of the 1920s a period of relative prosperity that historians call the "Golden Twenties" (Goldene Zwanziger). Berlin became a center of art, culture, science, and modern living. The new middle classes began buying radios and spending weekends at vacation resorts. Economic freedom and cultural expression reached dimensions in Weimar Berlin that had no precedent in Germany.

Political Consequences of Stabilization

Economic stabilization had a direct political effect: extremist parties – both the Nazis on the right and Communists on the left – lost part of their electorate because the atmosphere of crisis from which they fed was dissolving. The NSDAP did poorly in the 1924 and 1928 elections, and many commentators at the time believed the crisis had overcome Hitlerism. Weimar democracy seemed consolidated. It turned out that prosperity was needed not just for economic freedom, but for the survival of political freedom – and that their fate was intertwined in a way that could only fully manifest in the next moment of crisis, which came in 1929.
