13. MAY 1936 – State Defense Act

On May 13, 1936, the National Assembly passed Law No. 131/1936 Coll. on the Defense of the State, one of the most extensive legislative acts of the First Republic. The law resulted from two years of preparation and responded to a dramatically worsened security situation in Europe—the remilitarization of the Rhineland, the increase in German armaments, and the destabilization of Central Europe. The text was preceded by secret military command analyses showing that Czechoslovakia was incapable on its own of withstanding a German offensive without a fundamental modernization of its defense system.

What the Law Brought

The State Defense Act provided a comprehensive legal framework for preparing the country for war. It allowed the government in times of threat to take control of private enterprises and requisition property for military purposes. It imposed obligations on industrial enterprises to participate in weapons production and gave the army powers that in normal times belonged to civilian authorities. The law also permitted the restriction of freedom of movement, assembly, and press in areas declared to be threatened.

Freedom vs. Security

The State Defense Act was a classic example of tension between individual freedom and collective security. Its adoption was accompanied by practical agreement across political parties—even the opposition understood that without a defense-capable army there would be no one to protect freedom. At the same time, the law by its scope and generality granted the government extraordinary tools whose potential for abuse was obvious. Under conditions of stable democracy it was an acceptable compromise; under conditions of a totalitarian state the same text would become a weapon against the people. This ambivalence is characteristic of security legislation in general.

Real Results

The law enabled acceleration of border fortification construction (the so-called Czechoslovak Maginot Line), which covered hundreds of kilometers of border with Germany and Austria. It increased spending on the army, modernization of the air force, and armor. By 1938, Czechoslovakia had achieved relatively high military preparedness—paradoxically, it was forced by Munich to capitulate without a fight. The law thus fulfilled its technical purpose, but political reality overrode it.
