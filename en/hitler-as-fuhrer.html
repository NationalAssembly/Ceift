2. AUGUST 1934 – Hitler as Führer

On the morning of August 2, 1934, at four o'clock, President Paul von Hindenburg died on his estate in Neudeck at the age of eighty-eight. That same day, Hitler issued a law merging the offices of Reich President and Reich Chancellor into one. He became "Führer und Reichskanzler" – Leader and Reich Chancellor. All armed forces took a personal oath of loyalty directly to Hitler that same day – not to the constitution, state, or nation, but to one specific person.

The Last Constitutional Safeguard Removed

Hindenburg's presence as head of state had been the last remnant of the constitutional order of the Weimar Republic since 1933. The president theoretically had the right to dismiss the chancellor; the military had sworn to him, not Hitler. Hindenburg did not exercise these powers, and did not exercise them even when it was most urgently needed – in January 1933, when he appointed Hitler chancellor. But his existence as an office at least formally indicated that Hitler was not absolute sovereign. After August 2, 1934, this formal safeguard disappeared. There was no institution, no office, no law that could limit or revoke Hitler's power.

Plebiscite as Legitimization

To give the merger of offices an appearance of democratic legitimacy, a referendum was held on August 19, 1934. More than ninety percent of voters, under conditions of Nazi terror, without free media and without any opposition, approved Hitler's merger of offices. This vote was a facade of democracy without its substance – a ritual that demonstrated how empty the procedure of elections becomes when deprived of the freedom to vote differently. The Weimar Republic ceased to exist not only de facto but also de jure.
