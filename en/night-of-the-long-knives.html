30. JUNE 1934 – Night of the Long Knives

The turn of June and July 1934 brought events that shook even the Nazi movement itself and definitively showed what kind of power Germany was dealing with. Between June 30 and July 2, 1934, Hitler carried out a purge on an order given orally, in which over eighty people were murdered – most notably Ernst Röhm, commander of the SA, and conservative politicians such as former Chancellor Kurt von Schleicher. The action became known as the "Night of the Long Knives," and Hitler subsequently legalized it ex post with a law declaring the murders to be "necessary self-defense of the state."

Why the Purge Happened

Röhm and the SA leadership demanded continuation of the "second revolution," which would truly socialize the economy and incorporate the SA into a new people's army in place of the old Reichswehr. The Wehrmacht and industrial elites wanted the opposite – the SA under control and no social revolution. Hitler chose allies in the army and industry because he needed their support for rearmament and foreign expansion. Röhm and his "leftist" wing of the SA was sacrificed as a political necessity.

Murder as a Political Tool

The Night of the Long Knives was not merely a purge within the party – it was a public declaration that in Hitler's Germany there was no legal guarantee even for the Führer's closest collaborators. If Röhm could be murdered without trial, if Schleicher could be shot in his own home and the murders then declared legal – then truly no law applies, only the will of the Führer. This message was well understood: the Reichswehr swore loyalty to Hitler, industrialists accepted new cooperation terms, the public learned to be silent. Political terror ceased to be an exception and became the norm of governance.
