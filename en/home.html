CENTRAL EUROPE INTERWAR FREEDOM TIMELINE

Discover the history of freedom in Central Europe after World War I through an interactive Czech-German timeline and articles!

About the Project

CEIFT was created as part of the Czech-German student project Svodoba–Freiheit–Freedom between the Prague Gymnasium Jana Keplera and the Dresden Gymnasium Bürgerwiese.

CEIFT aims to document the history of freedom in Central Europe during the interwar period, using an interactive timeline with German events at the top and Czechoslovak events at the bottom. From the events on the timeline, links lead to short thematic articles supplemented with additional historical materials. The articles are complemented by a podcast about the Weimar National Assembly, placed below.

Podcast about the Weimar National Assembly

Podcast transcript

Gawa: Hello and welcome to our podcast. Today we will be talking about the Weimar National Assembly of 1919. This event was a very important moment in German history, as it led to the creation of the first parliamentary democracy in Germany. In this episode, we would like to answer the question: How did democracy develop in Germany after the First World War?


Livia: To understand the National Assembly, we must first look at the situation in Germany at the end of the First World War in 1918. Germany had lost the war and was facing many serious problems. The economy was unstable, food was scarce, and soldiers and citizens alike were dissatisfied with the political system.


Gawa: At that time, Germany was still a monarchy ruled by Kaiser Wilhelm II. Many people blamed the Kaiser and the imperial government for the war and the catastrophic situation in the country. Hunger and exhaustion, military defeat, and a loss of trust eventually led to the November Revolution of 1918, when a republic was proclaimed in Berlin.


Livia: During the revolution, workers, soldiers, and citizens protested against the monarchy. They demanded political change, a better economy, and more rights for the people. Under all this pressure, Wilhelm II abdicated and left the country. As a result, the monarchy ended and Germany became a republic.


Gawa: Yes, and Germany now needed a new political system. The government decided that the German people should be able to elect their representatives, who would together write a new constitution. The elections were held on 19 January 1919.


Livia: These elections were the first fully democratic national elections in Germany with equal voting rights for men and women. Another historic change, which has already been mentioned, was that women were allowed to vote and stand for election for the first time in history. It was a major step towards equality and democratic participation.


Gawa: The elected representatives formed the National Assembly. Notably, the assembly did not meet in Berlin, as you might have expected, but in Weimar, at the National Theatre. Berlin was still experiencing political unrest, protests, and violence. Weimar was chosen because it was known at the time as a much calmer and safer place.


Livia: The main task of the assembly was to create a new constitution for Germany. It was to define how the new state – later known as the "Weimar Republic" – would function. The so-called "Weimar Constitution" came into force on 11 August 1919.


Gawa: From that point on, the government was to be accountable to parliament and representatives were to be elected by citizens. All citizens over the age of 20 were allowed to vote, including women. This was very progressive for the time.


Livia: Power was divided among several institutions: the Reichstag approved laws, the government implemented policy, the Reich President served as head of state, and independent courts administered justice. The constitution guaranteed a number of rights, such as freedom of speech, freedom of assembly, and freedom of religion.


Gawa: The Reich President was elected by the people and had considerable powers, including appointing or dismissing the government, dissolving the Reichstag, issuing emergency decrees in times of crisis, and invoking Article 48. Although Article 48 was intended for crisis situations, it later became one of the weaknesses of the Weimar Constitution.


Livia: Article 48 states that the Reich President is authorised to intervene if "public safety and order" in Germany is threatened. He could then: issue emergency decrees (for example, laws without parliamentary approval), restrict fundamental rights (freedom of the press and assembly), deploy the army to restore order, and overturn decisions of individual states.


Gawa: Germany now had a democratic political system with a parliament called the Reichstag and a president elected by the people. The first president of Germany was Friedrich Ebert, who played a key role in guiding the country through the difficult transition from monarchy to democracy.


Livia: With the adoption of the constitution, the Weimar Republic officially began. It was the first parliamentary democracy in Germany. However, the new republic faced many challenges from the very beginning.


Gawa: Germany had to deal with economic problems, political conflicts, and the aftermath of the war. Even so, the National Assembly of 1919 was a very important step towards democracy and political participation in Germany.


Livia: To summarise, the Weimar National Assembly played a crucial role in shaping modern German democracy. It created new political rights and marked the beginning of a new era for Germany.
