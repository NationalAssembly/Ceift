29. SEPTEMBER 1938 – Munich Agreement

During the night of September 29-30, 1938, the leaders of the governments of Great Britain (Chamberlain), France (Daladier), Italy (Mussolini), and Germany (Hitler) signed in Munich an agreement by which Czechoslovakia, without its own participation, was forced to cede the Sudeten regions to Germany – border areas where the majority of Sudeten Germans lived but which also contained key industrial capacities, fortifications, and the republic's natural strategic borders. Prague was given an ultimatum: accept or face war without allied support. After agonizing overnight negotiations, Beneš accepted.

What Was Lost

The ceded territory encompassed approximately 29,000 km² and was inhabited by around 3.5 million people, of whom about 800,000 were Czechs. Along with the territory, the republic lost a considerable part of its border industry, nearly the entire network of border fortifications, and strategically crucial mountain passes. Economically and militarily, Czechoslovakia found itself in a situation with no way out: without mountains and fortifications, the remainder of the republic – ironically called the "Second Republic" – became indefensible. Over 200,000 Czechs, Germans, and Jews fled the borders to the interior.

Loss of Freedom as a Political System

The Munich Agreement was not merely a territorial loss. It was a violation of the democratic principle that small nations have the right to self-determination and that the fate of nations should be decided by international treaties, not by the diktat of powers. For Czechs and democratic Germans in the border regions, the advent of Nazi administration meant the immediate abolition of all freedoms: freedom of the press, assembly, and political activity. The Gestapo took over police headquarters, political parties were banned, and opponents' property was confiscated. Those who managed to escape lost their homes; those who remained and were not sufficiently "Aryan" in their loyalty soon lost their freedom or their lives.

The Munich Syndrome

The betrayal by allies – as Czechs perceived and called Munich – left a deep mark on collective memory. The conviction that "the West betrayed us" influenced Czech politics for decades: it contributed to the acceptance of Communist power after 1948 and to later arguments about the inevitability of dependence on the Soviet Union. The Munich Syndrome – distrust in the guarantees of democratic powers – was one of the most lasting damages that the agreement of September 1938 inflicted.
