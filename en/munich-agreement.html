The Munich Agreement – September 29–30, 1938

On September 29, 1938 (formally signed after midnight on September 30), Great Britain, France, Germany and Italy concluded an agreement in Munich that decided the fate of Czechoslovakia without its presence. The agreement was signed by Adolf Hitler, Neville Chamberlain, Édouard Daladier and Benito Mussolini. Chamberlain alone had spent a total of nine hours in negotiations with Hitler. Neither Czech nor Slovak representatives were invited to the talks – they were presented with the finished document and told to accept it. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Munich_Agreement [Wikipedia – Munich Agreement]

Terms and Conditions of the Transfer

The agreement ordered Czechoslovakia to evacuate the Sudeten German territories from October 1, with the evacuation to be completed by October 10. No installations on the transferred territory were to be destroyed – Czechoslovakia was therefore obliged to hand over intact its fortifications, industrial plants and infrastructure. Czecho-Slovakia lost more than a third of its territory, approximately 3.5 million inhabitants, the majority of its border fortifications and a significant share of its industrial capacity. The agreement also ordered the release of Sudeten German prisoners convicted of political offences. https://www.moderni-dejiny.cz/clanek/mnichovska-dohoda-29-9-1938/ [Moderní-Dějiny.cz – The Munich Agreement]

"About Us, Without Us" – The Diplomatic Context

The Munich Agreement is remembered in Czech historical memory as the "Munich betrayal" or "Munich dictate". Great Britain and France sacrificed their ally in the name of appeasement – the policy of conceding to Hitler's demands in the hope of preserving peace. A republic that had spent twenty years building a democratic state and possessed a functioning army and extensive fortifications was forced to surrender without a fight. Poland and Hungary immediately followed Germany's example, claiming their own territorial shares of the dismembered country. https://ct24.ceskatelevize.cz/clanek/domaci/mnichov-1938-nekteri-historici-odmitaji-tezi-o-zrade-slo-podle-nich-o-vitezstvi-benesovy-diplomacie-75427 [ČT24 – Munich 1938]

Immediate Consequences

President Edvard Beneš resigned following the acceptance of the Munich Agreement. Emil Hácha became the new president. The so-called Second Republic – Czecho-Slovakia – came into existence on reduced territory, struggling to cope with a flood of refugees from the ceded borderlands. The loss of the Sudeten borderlands also severed railway lines, as many of them ran through the newly detached territory. The mobilised army returned home without firing a shot. Despair and a sense of abandonment swept through society. https://www.umimefakta.cz/dejepis/cviceni-mnichovska-dohoda-a-druha-republika [Umíme fakta – The Munich Agreement and the Second Republic]

Historical Legacy

The Munich Agreement became a symbol of the failure of collective security and a warning against the policy of appeasement towards aggressive powers. It demonstrated that democratic states could be sacrificed by their allies without their own consent. Less than six months after Munich, on March 15, 1939, Nazi Germany occupied the remainder of Bohemia and Moravia, definitively burying the last remnants of independence. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Munich_Agreement [Wikipedia – Munich Agreement]
