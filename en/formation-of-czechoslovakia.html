28. OCTOBER 1918 – Formation of Czechoslovakia

On the morning of October 28, 1918, news arrived from Vienna that many found hard to believe: Austria-Hungary had agreed to the peace terms of the Allied powers and factually capitulated. The same day, the Czechoslovak National Committee in Prague announced the creation of an independent state. Thousands of people gathered on the steps of the Municipal House and at Wenceslas Square – people who had not dared to say aloud what they thought just weeks before. After three centuries of Habsburg rule and four years of war, came the day that was recorded as the Uprising – with a capital U it was written automatically.

Čtyři roky bez svobody

The war years 1914–1918 meant for the inhabitants of the Czech lands the systematic suppression of fundamental rights. Freedom of speech was abolished by censorship, freedom of movement by military control, freedom of association by bans on political activity. Newspapers could be published only if their content was approved by military censors. Anyone who spoke publicly against the war or the monarchy risked court-martial. Thousands of Czechs ended up in prison or on the gallows simply for refusing loyalty to an empire that did not regard them as equal citizens. It was from this environment that October 28, 1918, saw such a powerful wave of relief, expressed in massive crowds in the streets.

Okamžik vyhlášení

The National Committee, led by Antonín Švehla, Alois Rašín, František Soukup, Jiří Stříbrný, and Vavro Šrobár, acted quickly. Rašín, one of the authors of the proclamation, feared that any hesitation could ruin the situation. Law No. 11/1918 Coll., the first law of the new republic, took over all previous provincial and imperial legislation with the understanding that it would be gradually modified. Although it was a law of modest scope, it symbolically fixed the existence of the new state and guaranteed its legal continuity. The Slovak National Council declared Slovakia's accession to the new state two days later, on October 30, in Martin.

Co svoboda znamenala v praxi

For ordinary inhabitants of the Czech lands, freedom from October 28 was not merely a political abstraction. It meant that newspapers would no longer have blank spaces from the censor's pen. That they could attend political meetings without risking denunciation. That their fate would no longer be decided by Vienna authorities or military courts. These changes did not come overnight – the transitional period lasted months. But October 28 was a symbolic point zero, from which the new social contract began to be written. Freedom, which seemed unattainable under the Habsburg state, suddenly became the daily reality of institution-building.
