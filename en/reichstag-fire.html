27. FEBRUARY 1933 – Reichstag Fire

On the evening of February 27, 1933, the Reichstag in Berlin erupted in flames. Before the fire could be extinguished, the Nazis knew how to exploit the situation. Hitler, Göring, and Goebbels immediately declared the fire to be communist arson and a signal for revolutionary coup. A young Dutch communist, Marinus van der Lubbe, was arrested at the scene. It has not yet been proven with absolute certainty whether he acted alone or whether the Nazis staged or exploited the fire—historians still argue over this question. What is certain is the manner in which the fire was politically exploited.

The Event as Pretext

Still on the night of the fire, Hitler ordered mass arrests of communists, social democrats, and other opponents. Hundreds of deputies, union leaders, and journalists were dragged away to prison or improvised places of internment that night or in the following days. The Reichstag fire served as a catalyst that enabled the Nazis to move from legally undermining the democratic system to openly and violently smashing it.

Direct Impact on Freedoms

Even more important than the night of arrests was the legislative assault that immediately followed the fire. The Hindenburg Decree of February 28, 1933—issued the day after the fire at Hitler's suggestion—suspended key fundamental rights of the Weimar Constitution. The Reichstag fire thus did not act as a cause, but as a trigger: it enabled the Nazis to do under conditions of artificial crisis what would normally have required open confrontation with parliament and the public. In this sense, the fire—however it came about—was one of the most important events in the history of German freedom.
