1. NOVEMBER 1923 – Hyperinflation

The autumn of 1923 brought to Germany an economic apocalypse whose dimensions surpassed everything that modern industrial society had yet experienced. The value of the mark collapsed so thoroughly that in November 1923 it took over four trillion marks to buy a single dollar. Prices changed even in the course of a single day; people carried money in suitcases and wheelbarrows; bakers sold bread in exchange for goods because with paper money they would not be able to reach the till before it lost its value. An entire class of precisely those people who formed the foundation of democratic society – the middle class, clerks, teachers, pensioners – lost their lifetime savings literally overnight.

Loss of Economic Freedom as a Political Threat

Hyperinflation is not, at first glance, a matter of freedom, but its impact on individual freedom was far-reaching. A person who loses their financial security and faces existential anxiety every day has neither the mental nor material space to exercise political rights. Surveys from the time and later historical analyses show that it was precisely hyperinflation that sowed deep distrust in the Weimar Republic and the democratic system in general: it was a state that "allowed savings to collapse," it was a system that did not protect people from such disasters. This distrust was a reservoir of bitterness from which politicians of the far right and left drew for the entire 1920s.

Stabilization and Psychological Scar

Hyperinflation was eventually stopped by the introduction of the Rentenmark in November 1923 and the Dawes Plan in 1924. Economic stabilization came relatively quickly – but the psychological scar remained. The generation of Germans who lived through 1923 carried a deep fear of inflation that significantly influenced their behavior and political preferences even during World War II, and whose echoes are evident in German economic culture to this day. The Bundesbank and after it the European Central Bank are to a large extent products of the memory of 1923.
