29. AUGUST 1916 – Military Dictatorship

On August 29, 1916, Generals Paul von Hindenburg and Erich Ludendorff accepted appointment to the head of the Supreme Army Command (Oberste Heeresleitung – OHL). On paper, it was a change of command in the middle of the war. In reality, it was a quiet assumption of political power of a kind Germany had not experienced since Bismarck's era. The civilian government and parliament were sidelined, and Emperor William II, to whom the constitution formally entrusted supreme command, was becoming increasingly a mere figurehead with no real influence.

How the "Quiet Dictatorship" Arose

Hindenburg and Ludendorff did not carry out a coup with tanks on the streets. Their path to power was more subtle: they exploited a combination of dissatisfaction with the prevailing conduct of the war, their own popularity (Hindenburg's cult was at its height in 1916), and politicians' willingness to hand over responsibility to the military. Chancellor Bethmann Hollweg soon found himself in a position where he had to defer to military leadership in virtually all key decisions – from peace conditions to economic policy. Historians call this situation "quiet dictatorship" (stille Diktatur), although formally no law ever declared such a dictatorship.

Impact on Civil Liberties

The rise of Hindenburg and Ludendorff brought a perceptible tightening of wartime control of society. Censorship of the press became more stringent and systematic – the Kriegspresseamt (War Press Office) expanded its activities, suppressed information about losses, and shaped public opinion in the necessary direction. The right to assemble, debate peace terms, or criticize government policy became increasingly risky. The Auxiliary Military Service Law (Hilfsdienstgesetz) of December 1916, one of the first legislative results of the new military leadership, effectively militarized the civilian workforce and restricted workers' freedom to change jobs freely. The state ceased to be merely a warring nation and began transforming into an organism entirely subordinate to the war machine.

Legacy for Post-War Germany

Experience with military dictatorship left deep scars in German political culture. Military leadership became accustomed to intervening in civilian affairs and was slow to relinquish this habit. After defeat in 1918, Hindenburg and Ludendorff actively spread the lie of the "stab in the back" (Dolchstoßlegende), which shifted responsibility for the lost war onto civilians and the left. This legend then poisoned the atmosphere of the Weimar Republic and served as one of the ideological tools the Nazis exploited in their rise. The authoritarian style of governance that Hindenburg and Ludendorff normalized in 1916–1918 thus became a precedent of dangerous force.
