4. OCTOBER 1933 – Ban on Nazi Party

On October 4, 1933, the Czechoslovak government issued a decree dissolving the German National Socialist Workers' Party (DNSAP) and its affiliated Deutsche Nationalpartei (DNP). This was a direct application of the Law for the Protection of the Republic and one of the first actions by a democratic state in Central Europe against a party that had adopted Hitlerism as its political program. The assets of both parties were confiscated, their publications banned, and leading functionaries faced criminal prosecution.

What the DNSAP Represented

The DNSAP had existed in Czechoslovakia intermittently since the 1920s and maintained close contacts with the NSDAP in Germany. After Hitler's rise to power in January 1933, this connection deepened: the party adopted Nazi salutes, symbols, and propaganda, openly called for the incorporation of the Sudeten regions into the German Reich, and spread antisemitic content in its publications. Through its paramilitary organizations (Volkssport), it attempted to create structures similar to the SA. For a republic that guaranteed freedom of political expression and association, this posed an extraordinarily difficult test: where does tolerance end?

The Law in Practice

The government relied on § 4 of the Law for the Protection of the Republic, which allowed it to dissolve an organization that "threatens the constitutional order, republican or democratic form of the state, or the integrity or security of the state." The DNSAP clearly met these criteria: it collaborated with a foreign power, incited the secession of border territories, and denied the fundamental principles of democratic governance on which the republic was founded. By banning it, the party lost its formal existence, but not its adherents – they soon joined the newly established Sudeten German Party (SdP) of Konrad Henlein, which appeared more moderate but in fact pursued similar goals.

Freedom as Paradox

The ban on the DNSAP was symbolically important: it showed that the republic would not tolerate its own destruction under the guise of freedom of association. At the same time, it demonstrated the fragility of the situation: banning one party did not solve the problem, only shifted it. A real response to Nazi agitation might have been socioeconomic policies that offered border Germans better prospects – but the state lacked both the capacity and political will for such policies. With the ban on the DNSAP, the republic won a battle, but it did not stop the war.
