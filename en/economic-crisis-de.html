1. APRIL 1932 – Economic Crisis

The Great Economic Crisis struck Germany with a time lag after the New York stock market crash in October 1929, but once it hit, it struck in a devastating way. April 1932 was the statistical peak: nearly six million registered unemployed, to which must be added millions more working part-time or in irregular employment. More than forty percent of the able-bodied population had no work. Industrial production had collapsed to half the values from 1928. Germany, which as recently as 1929 seemed to be on the path to stability and prosperity, had plunged into the deepest economic crisis of its modern history.

Poverty as Loss of Freedom

Mass unemployment and poverty had a direct impact on freedom in its most basic, existential dimension. Millions of people lost their homes, sustained themselves in soup kitchens, and slept in shelters. The social benefits that the Weimar Republic had built since its founding were not sufficient to cover the needs of such a large number of unemployed. The government cut spending—and each cut meant that someone lost their eligibility for benefits, child allowances, health care. The freedom of choice—what to eat, where to live, what to study, whom to vote for—was narrowed for much of the population to the question of physical survival. Under such conditions, there is little room for substantive political deliberation and democratic decision-making.

Radicalization and Erosion of Democracy

The crisis dramatically strengthened extremist parties. The NSDAP, which had obtained just over two percent of the vote in 1928, jumped to 37 percent in the July 1932 elections—the largest result in the party's history in democratic elections. The Communist Party grew as well, though more slowly. The democratic center was crumbling. Democratic governance became increasingly difficult: parliament did not function, chancellors ruled on the basis of presidential emergency decrees, elections were held in rapid succession without producing a functioning majority. April 1932 was thus not only the statistical peak of the economic crisis, but also the moment when it became clear that free democracy in Germany was in critical danger.
