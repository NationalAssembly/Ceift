14. MARCH 1939 – Secession of Slovakia

On March 14, 1939, the Slovak parliament in Bratislava proclaimed the creation of an independent Slovak State. The declaration took place under dramatic circumstances: Jozef Tiso had been summoned to Berlin the day before, where Hitler informed him that if Slovakia did not declare independence immediately, he would allow Hungary to swallow it. Tiso returned, the parliament convened in haste, and voted. It was an act under direct Nazi pressure, although Slovak autonomists had indeed been seeking their own state.

A Long Road to Autonomy and Dependence

The demand for Slovak autonomy was not new. The Pittsburgh Agreement of 1918 promised Slovaks autonomous arrangements within a common state, which however never fully materialized in reality. Prague approached Slovakia paternalistically: Czech officials filled Slovak positions, and while the Slovak language and culture were tolerated, political and economic power remained centralized. This discontent fueled the autonomy movement of Hlinka's Slovak People's Party (HSĽS), which from the 1930s increasingly openly spoke of its own state.

Freedom or Vassalage?

The Slovak State soon proved to be not a free state, but a satellite of the Third Reich. On March 23, 1939, Hitler issued the so-called Schutzvertrag—a protective treaty—by which he practically annexed Slovak foreign and defense policy. German military units operated on Slovak territory, Slovak industry was integrated into the German war economy, and the Jozef Tiso regime systematically eliminated political opposition. The persecution of Jews in Slovakia ended in deportations from 1942. The state, which appeared to be the result of Slovak longing for freedom, was in reality a tool of Nazi dominance.

The End of the Common State

For Czechs, March 14, 1939, was another stone on the path to complete loss of statehood that came the next day with the arrival of the German army in Prague. For democrats in Slovakia—communists, social democrats, liberals—the declaration of the Slovak State was the beginning of repression. The freedom that Hlinka's followers spoke of was soon transformed into an authoritarian clerical state with one permitted party and persecution of those who thought differently. The demise of Czechoslovakia was thus not only a territorial and political, but also a moral catastrophe.
