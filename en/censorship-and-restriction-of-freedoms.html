CENSORSHIP OF THE PRESS AND RESTRICTION OF ASSEMBLY – 31. JULY 1914

On July 31, 1914, the German Empire declared the so-called "state of war danger" (Kriegsgefahrenzustand). This step, taken merely a day before the official declaration of war on Russia, represented the first and immediate blow struck against the body of civil freedoms that Germans had gradually built during Bismarck's and Wilhelm's eras. The decree did not arise from parliamentary debate or public discussion—it was simply issued by the military-bureaucratic apparatus, which saw the war as a matter too important to be disturbed by dissenting voices.

Immediate Censorship as the First Measure

Immediately after the declaration of Kriegsgefahrenzustand, strict press censorship was introduced. Every periodical had to submit texts for approval by military censors before it could be printed. Publishing reports that might endanger "the security of the empire" or the war effort was forbidden under threat of criminal prosecution. Newspapers across the political spectrum—from the conservative Kreuzzeitung to the Social Democratic Vorwärts—suddenly found themselves in a situation where telling the truth was legally dangerous and silence became a comfortable survival strategy. Editorials that in summer 1914 were still reporting on August euphoria and flag-waving crowds soon discovered that enthusiasm for war was acceptable, but doubts were criminal.

Restriction of Assembly and Political Activity

Concurrent with censorship, the right to assembly was drastically restricted. Public meetings, demonstrations, and association gatherings now required explicit permission from authorities. Political parties, trade unions, and associations that before the war had formed a vibrant part of German public life suddenly found themselves in the grip of emergency ordinances. Police and military authorities gained extensive powers to act against anything that showed even hints of "antiwar" or "destabilizing" sentiment. Social democracy—then the largest party in the Reichstag—found itself in a paradoxical situation: its parliamentary faction voted for war loans to avoid endangering its legal existence, while party leaders knew that internal war opponents would be under constant surveillance.

The Beginning of Authoritarian Control of Society

This first wave of restrictions is historically crucial because it laid the institutional foundation for the much harsher wartime dictatorship that developed in Germany in the following years. Germans who as recently as July 1914 lived in a state with a relatively vibrant parliamentary culture suddenly found themselves in a regime where freedom of speech, information, and public expression ceased to be protected values and became subject to state arbitrariness. It was precisely this experience—that guarantees hold only until the government decides otherwise—that had far-reaching psychological and political consequences in Germany, persisting long after the armistice was signed.
