23. SEPTEMBER 1938 – General Mobilization

On September 23, 1938, at 22:30, a voice rang out on the radio that changed the atmosphere of the entire country: the president of the republic announced general mobilization. Conscripts were given orders to report to barracks within hours. Over a million men responded, and over the following day the army occupied fortifications on the borders. The mobilization did not cause panic – instead, it provoked a wave of patriotic determination that surprised observers both at home and abroad with its spontaneity and calm.

Hodina odhodlání

The mobilization came at a moment when diplomatic tensions had reached the breaking point. British Prime Minister Chamberlain had just returned from talks with Hitler in Bad Godesberg, where Hitler was raising his demands far beyond acceptability. Beneš understood that without a clear signal of determination to defend, the republic would lose even the remnants of international support. Mobilization was that signal – a demonstration that Czechoslovakia was not willing to capitulate without a fight. The soldiers who went to the barracks brought food supplies, said goodbye to their families, and yet went willingly and without hesitation.

Svoboda jako hodnota, pro niž stojí za to bojovat

The mobilization of September 23, 1938, is remembered in Czech and Slovak history as evidence that freedom was not merely an abstract value for the republic's inhabitants. Those who in 1918 had gained the right to vote, work freely, and live without fear of military courts were willing to defend these achievements with weapons in hand. This resolve, however, ran up against the reality of international politics, which decided the republic's fate without its participation.

Mnichovská past

Mobilization ended – without a single shot fired and without victory – a week later. The Munich Agreement of September 29–30, 1938, forced Czechoslovakia to surrender its border regions, fortifications, and a third of its territory. Soldiers returned home without having been able to fight. For many of them, this was a more bitter disappointment than military defeat: they were prepared to die for the republic, but no one gave them that opportunity. This experience struck the nation's collective memory in a way whose consequences are evident to this day.
