11. JANUARY 1923 – Occupation of the Rhineland

On January 11, 1923, French and Belgian troops entered the industrial heartland of Germany – the Ruhr. The pretext was alleged non-fulfillment of war reparations set by the Treaty of Versailles: Germany supposedly had not delivered agreed amounts of wood and telegraph poles. Critics then and now point out that the real reason was French interest in direct control over German industry and attempts to secure reparations by physical appropriation of industrial production. In any case, the result was that a foreign army occupied a region with more than five million inhabitants and key factories, mines, and foundries.

Passive Resistance and Its Cost

The German government declared a policy of passive resistance – workers were urged not to cooperate with the occupiers, businesses stopped delivering, civil servants refused to obey orders from French authorities. The state paid striking workers from an empty treasury, thereby setting in motion a spiral of hyperinflation of unprecedented dimensions. The result was that while passive resistance demonstrated national unity and defiance, its economic costs were catastrophic. When Chancellor Gustav Stresemann ended the resistance in September 1923, the price of bread was measured in billions of marks and the middle class was losing its lifetime savings.

Loss of State Sovereignty as Loss of Freedom

The occupation of the Rhineland struck at German freedoms in their state dimension: when a foreign army controls part of a state's territory, the freedom of its citizens in that territory is at best limited and at worst dependent on the whim of the occupiers. In the occupied areas, orders were issued by French military authorities, some Germans were deported for resistance, and newspapers were subject to French censorship. For the inhabitants of the Ruhr, this was not an abstract geopolitical question – it was the daily experience of subjection to foreign power on one's own territory.

Political Consequences: Radicalization

The crisis of 1923 – a combination of occupation, hyperinflation, and national humiliation – created ideal ground for extremist parties at both ends of the spectrum. In Bavaria, Adolf Hitler exploited the atmosphere of crisis for his Beer Hall Putsch in November 1923. Communists attempted revolutionary seizure of power in Saxony and Thuringia. The close link between economic catastrophe, loss of national sovereignty, and the rise of political extremism is abundantly clear in 1923 – and it is a link that repeated with grim regularity throughout the interwar period.
