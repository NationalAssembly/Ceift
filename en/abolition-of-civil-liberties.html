28. FEBRUARY 1933 – Abolition of Civil Liberties

On February 28, 1933, the day after the Reichstag Fire, President Hindenburg signed the decree "For the Protection of People and State" (Verordnung zum Schutz von Volk und Staat). This decree, issued under Article 48 of the Weimar Constitution, effectively abolished the entire second part of the constitution dedicated to fundamental rights with a single stroke of the pen. The freedoms that Germans had built since 1919 were suspended indefinitely.

What Was Abolished

The decree suspended freedom of speech, press, assembly, and association. The inviolability of the home, postal secrecy, and protection of personal freedom were revoked. Police (and soon SA, SS, and Gestapo) could arrest and detain people without a court order, search apartments, ban newspapers, and prohibit political parties. These measures were technically temporary under law, but they were never rescinded – they remained in effect until the end of the Third Reich in 1945. Article 48, which had been included in the constitution as insurance for genuine emergencies, was used as a tool for permanent destruction of the legal state meant to prevent such emergencies.

Mass Arrests and Terror

Immediately after the decree's issuance, the machinery of repression began. The Communist Party was effectively banned even before the March elections, with thousands of its members and functionaries ending up in detention. Social Democratic MPs faced intimidation, threats, and physical attacks. Newspapers critical of the Nazi movement were shut down one after another. Improvised detention camps – predecessors of later concentration camps – rapidly filled with political opponents. This terror occurred before the March elections, at a time when the German public still formally had the right to freely decide the fate of its state.

Legal Basis of Dictatorship

Hindenburg's decree is one of the most important documents in the modern history of freedom in Germany – not for what it introduced, but for what it abolished. It demonstrated how easily constitutionally enshrined rights can be removed if conditions are favorable and the will to resist is lacking. The lesson was learned: the authors of the Basic Law of the Federal Republic of Germany after 1949 therefore enshrined that fundamental rights are inviolable – and included no equivalent to Article 48 in the new constitution.
