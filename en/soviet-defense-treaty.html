16. MAY 1935 – Soviet Defense Treaty

On May 16, 1935, the Treaty of Mutual Assistance between Czechoslovakia and the Soviet Union was signed in Prague. Foreign Minister Edvard Beneš and Soviet People's Commissar Maxim Litvinov appended their signatures to a document designed to guarantee Soviet military assistance in the event that Germany attacked Czechoslovakia—but on the condition that assistance would first be activated by France, with which a Franco-Soviet treaty had been signed a day earlier.

Why the Treaty at All

An alliance with the totalitarian Soviet state was for democratic Czechoslovakia an ideologically compromising but strategically understandable step. Nazi Germany had represented an increasing military threat since 1933, traditional alliance with France seemed insufficiently firm, and Britain had turned to a policy of appeasement. Beneš sought insurance, and the USSR—then still a member of the League of Nations—offered it. The treaty built on an earlier concept of collective security, designed to preserve the Versailles system and with it the security framework without which the freedom of small Central European states could not survive.

Criticism and Controversy

The treaty aroused controversy from several quarters. Right-wing and Christian parties in Czechoslovakia warned against rapprochement with Bolshevik Russia and ideological contamination of foreign policy. Sudeten Germans and Hungarians rejected it as a provocative step. Germany labeled it an encircling policy and used it as a propaganda argument. Internationally, the treaty contributed to tensions that Beneš was unable to compensate for by strengthening ties to the West. It turned out that an alliance conditional on activation by a third party was in times of crisis practically worthless—as September 1938 proved.

Freedom on a Thin Thread

The treaty with the USSR illustrates the paradox into which Czechoslovakia had gotten itself in the 1930s: in order to defend its liberal democracy, it had to rely on a state that at the same time was carrying out Stalinist purges, collectivization, and the starvation of millions. The republic's freedom depended on the guarantees of powers—and these proved to be either inadequate or conditional on foreign will. The May 1935 treaty was thus not merely a diplomatic document, but a warning signal of the international isolation of a small democratic state in an increasingly hostile European environment.
