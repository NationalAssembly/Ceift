14. SEPTEMBER 1937 – Death of Masaryk

On Thursday, September 14, 1937, in the early morning hours, Tomáš Garrigue Masaryk died in Lány at the age of 87. A man passed whose name was for millions of inhabitants of the republic a synonym not just for the state but for an ideal: truth, democracy, humanism. The news spread quickly through the country and the public reaction was unprecedented. Thousands of people spontaneously came to the funeral sites, newspapers issued extraordinary edition after extraordinary edition, and condolences came from every corner of the world.

Masaryk's Legacy for Freedom

Masaryk was not merely the founder of the state in a political sense. He was a thinker whose lifelong work was an argument for freedom as a value without which modern humans cannot live with dignity. As a professor of philosophy at Prague University, he stood against antisemitism during the Hilsner affair (1899–1900), when he risked his career and physical safety. As a deputy in the Imperial Parliament, he fought for freedom of the press and political equality. As president, he built a state in which freedom was not merely a slogan but the daily practice of institutions.

Death at a Time of Threat

Masaryk's passing came at the most sensitive possible moment. Germany was rearming, Henlein's SdP was gaining strength in the border regions, and the international system of collective security was slowly collapsing. Those who knew Masaryk personally were aware that the republic was losing its moral safeguard – a person whose authority transcended party interests and whose word carried weight even on the international stage. Beneš was a good political successor, but he never achieved Masaryk's non-political authority.

The End of an Era

Masaryk's death closed an era in which the democratic republic of Czechs and Slovaks drew legitimacy also from the personality of its founder. After 1937, the state was led by a diplomat without Masaryk's moral and symbolic power. Many citizens felt this as a weakening – and as Munich a year later showed, their intuition was not wrong. Masaryk's death was not the cause of the republic's fall, but its sad portent.
