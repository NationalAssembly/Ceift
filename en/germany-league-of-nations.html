8. SEPTEMBER 1926 – Germany Member of the League of Nations

On September 8, 1926, Germany was admitted to the League of Nations and immediately received a permanent seat on the Council – alongside Great Britain, France, Italy, and Japan. This act had profound symbolic and practical dimensions. Symbolically: Germany, which had been treated as responsible for the war and excluded from international structures after 1918, was now accepted as an equal partner in the key forum of international law. Practically: admission gave Germany a platform on which it could pursue a peaceful revision of the disadvantageous terms of the Treaty of Versailles.

Stresemann in Geneva

At the ceremonial reception, Stresemann delivered a speech in which he emphasized Germany's commitment to peace and cooperation, but also subtly indicated that the republic expected concrete results from membership in the League – particularly regarding the adjustment of reparations, the withdrawal of military control commissions, and the gradual end of Versailles dictatorship. This approach – staying within the system but persistently working to change it through diplomatic means – was the essence of Stresemann's policy and earned him respect in both Paris and London.

Membership and Internal Freedom

Admission to the League of Nations had a direct impact on the internal political climate of the Weimar Republic. The nationalist right, which regarded Germany as defeated and humiliated, lost part of its argument when it became clear that a policy of understanding was producing concrete results. The German Democratic Party and liberal circles could point to success as proof that democracy was a better strategy for Germany than revanchism. In the short term, this strengthened democratic forces within the republic – and thereby indirectly protected the freedoms Germans had enjoyed since 1919.
