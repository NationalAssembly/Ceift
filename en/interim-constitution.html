13. NOVEMBER 1918 – Interim Constitution

Sixteen days after independence was proclaimed, the Revolutionary National Assembly convened for another fundamental step: approval of the interim constitution of the Czechoslovak Republic. Law No. 37/1918 Coll. was passed on November 13, 1918, and as the first document of its kind, it defined the basic rules for the functioning of the new state. It was not yet a definitive constitutional text, but a working framework that was to provide democratic arrangements with institutions before a proper constitution came into being.

New Order Instead of Habsburg Rules

The interim constitution abolished the old imperial structures and replaced them with a new organization of power. It entrusted legislative power to the Revolutionary National Assembly, executive power to the government and the president of the republic. In this way, Czechoslovakia clearly distinguished itself from the Habsburg model, where executive power rested in the hands of the emperor and his appointed ministers. The new system enshrined parliamentary control of government and the principles of democratic governance, albeit in a provisional, incomplete form. It was the first institutional expression of sovereignty that could be explained to every citizen.

Voting Rights for Women – A Historical Breakthrough

One of the most remarkable aspects of the interim constitution was the introduction of universal and equal voting rights without distinction of gender. Women, to whom the Austro-Hungarian monarchy had completely denied any political rights, overnight became full voters. With this step, Czechoslovakia joined the pioneering states of Europe – women's suffrage was introduced at that time by, for example, Finland (1906) and Germany (1918), while France waited until 1944 and Switzerland until 1971. The interim constitution thus signaled that the new state did not intend to inherit the inequalities of the old regime but wanted to commit itself to modern democratic principles.

The Fragility of a New Order

Although the law was passed unanimously and with enthusiasm, the interim constitution naturally did not resolve everything. Questions of minority rights, the relationship between Czechs and Slovaks, the nature of military conscription, or the structure of the judiciary remained open. The Revolutionary National Assembly was an appointed body, not an elected one, and its democratic legitimacy was therefore limited. Nevertheless, November 13, 1918, was the day when freedom ceased to be merely a proclamation from the street and became part of the legal order.
