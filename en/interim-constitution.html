November 13, 1918 – Adoption of the Provisional Constitution

On November 13, 1918, the National Committee approved the Act on the Provisional Constitution of the Czechoslovak Republic. Just sixteen days after the declaration of independence, this document laid the foundations of the new state order and brought a sweeping expansion of civil liberties – replacing the centralised Habsburg system with a democratic framework built from the ground up. https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prozat%C3%ADmn%C3%AD_%C3%BDstava_%C4%8Ceskoslovenska [Wikipedia – Provisional Constitution of Czechoslovakia]

Women's Suffrage – Czechoslovakia Among the First

One of the most significant elements of the provisional constitution was the introduction of universal suffrage, including the equal right of women to vote and to stand for election. Czechoslovakia thus ranked among the first countries in Europe to fully open the doors of politics to women. Austria-Hungary had systematically excluded women from political decision-making; the new state granted them full civic equality in electoral rights from the very outset – ahead of many Western European democracies. https://www.psp.cz/sqw/hp.sqw?k=697 [Parliament of the Czech Republic – History of Parliamentarism]

Structure of the New State

The provisional constitution defined the powers of the National Assembly, which assumed legislative authority. It established the office of President of the Republic – Tomáš Garrigue Masaryk was elected the first president by the National Assembly on November 14, 1918, the very day after the constitution was adopted. The government was made accountable to parliament, marking a decisive break from the Habsburg model in which the emperor held supreme executive power and ministers answered to him rather than to elected representatives. https://www.hrad.cz/cs/prezident-cr/historie-uradujicich-prezidentu/tomas-garrigue-masaryk [Prague Castle – Tomáš Garrigue Masaryk]

Breaking with the Habsburg Order

Under Austria-Hungary, political power had been concentrated in Vienna, and many basic freedoms – freedom of the press, assembly and association – had been severely curtailed, especially after the declaration of martial law in 1914. The provisional constitution swept away this legacy. For the citizens of the Czech lands and Slovakia, it represented an immediate and tangible transformation: rights that had been denied or suppressed for years were now guaranteed by law. https://www.dejepis.com/ucebnice/vznik-ceskoslovenska/ [Dejepis.com – Formation of Czechoslovakia]

A Stepping Stone to the 1920 Constitution

The provisional constitution was always intended as a temporary measure – a framework to govern the new state while a permanent constitutional order was being drafted. It fulfilled this role successfully, providing democratic legitimacy and institutional stability during the turbulent first months of independence. It was replaced by the permanent Constitution of the Czechoslovak Republic, adopted on February 29, 1920, which confirmed and deepened the democratic principles first enshrined in November 1918. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Czechoslovakia [Wikipedia – Constitution of Czechoslovakia]
