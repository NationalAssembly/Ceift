30. MAY 1917 – Political Concessions

After more than three years of de facto government absolutism, on May 30, 1917, the doors of the Imperial Council in Vienna opened again. Emperor Charles I, who came to the throne following the death of Franz Joseph I in November 1916, decided to restore parliamentary activity, which had been effectively frozen since the outbreak of war in August 1914. This session was the first public political forum in which deputies—including Czech ones—could once again openly formulate their demands. It was the first visible crack in the system of wartime repression that had gripped the monarchy for four years.

Wartime Silence in Parliament

The Habsburg monarchy entered the world war with a parliament that was effectively taken out of service. Prime Minister Count Stürgkh had already adjourned the Imperial Council in March 1914, and no further sessions were called after the conflict broke out. The monarchy thus functioned exclusively on the basis of imperial decrees, with §14 of the December constitution allowing the government to issue ordinances with the force of law without parliamentary consent. For Czech deputies, this meant the loss of the only legal space in which it was possible to articulate national demands. Press censorship, military courts, and systematic persecution of any opposition shaped the image of a monarchy that understood the war as a pretext for suppressing everything that might challenge Viennese centralism.

Amnesty and First Voices of Freedom

The restoration of parliament was accompanied by a partial amnesty of political prisoners. Released was, among others, Karel Kramář, leader of the Young Czech Party, who had been sentenced to death for treason in 1916—though the sentence was commuted, he had spent over a year in prison. It was Kramář who, after his release, became one of the loudest critics of wartime policy in the restored parliament. Czech deputies came forward with the Declaration of the Czech Union of May 30, 1917, in which they demanded the transformation of the Habsburg Empire into a federal state and the establishment of an independent state of Czechs and Slovaks within the monarchy. While this document was still loyal to the dynasty, it represented the first publicly articulated program of national autonomy since the beginning of the war.

A Shift in Political Atmosphere

The restoration of parliament in itself did not bring true freedom. Censorship persisted, military courts continued to function, and the government retained extensive emergency powers. Nevertheless, it was a turning point: politicians returned to the public sphere, the press once again had something to report on, and society felt that the absolute wartime stranglehold was loosening. In the Czech context, this step initiated a process that led—though more than a year later—to the declaration of an independent state. The restoration of parliament thus did not represent a return to freedom, but the first glimmer of what seemed to be possible.
