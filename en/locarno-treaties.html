1. DECEMBER 1925 – Locarno Treaties

The summer and autumn of 1925 brought a rare moment of goodwill to European diplomacy. In Swiss Locarno, representatives of Germany, France, Belgium, Great Britain, and Italy met and negotiated a set of treaties designed to stabilize post-war Europe and overcome the trauma of the Treaty of Versailles. The ceremonial signing took place on December 1, 1925, in London. The key result was a guarantee of the inviolability of borders between Germany, France, and Belgium – and a German pledge to resolve disputed questions through arbitration rather than force.

Stresemann and the Policy of Understanding

The Locarno Treaties were a success for Foreign Minister Gustav Stresemann, who became the symbol of Weimar foreign policy. Stresemann was a realistic politician aware of German weakness, but also deeply convinced that a revision of Versailles – without which there would be no lasting internal stability in Germany – had to come through diplomatic channels and international cooperation, not confrontation. Locarno earned him the Nobel Peace Prize (shared with French minister Briand) and the status of a statesman who had brought Germany back into the family of nations.

Freedom in an International Framework

From a freedom perspective, the Locarno Treaties are interesting in that they show how closely the freedom of a small or medium-sized state is connected to the international order in which it exists. The Weimar Republic needed foreign policy stability to maintain its democratic institutions – without it, internal pressure from extremists could more easily overcome democratic safeguards. Locarno provided this stability for approximately five years. It was a window in which the Weimar Republic could consolidate – and many historians mark this very window as the time when democracy in Germany had its best chance of surviving.

Unfinished Business: Eastern Borders

At the same time, the Locarno Treaties carried a politically explosive gap: while Germany's western borders were internationally guaranteed, its borders with Poland and Czechoslovakia were not. This was acceptable to German right-wing politicians as a signal that a revision of the eastern borders remained an open question – but for Poland and Czechoslovakia it was a troubling signal about the reliability of any security architecture that included Germany.
