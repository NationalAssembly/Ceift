Kristallnacht – November 9–10, 1938

In the night of November 9–10, 1938, organised pogroms against the Jewish population broke out across Nazi Germany, Austria, the Sudetenland and Danzig, going down in history as Kristallnacht (Night of Broken Glass). The pretext was the attack by seventeen-year-old Herschel Grynszpan on the secretary of the German embassy in Paris, Ernst vom Rath – Grynszpan's revenge for his family's deportation to the Polish border. Nazi propaganda immediately exploited the act to unleash organised terror, presented to the outside world as a spontaneous outburst of popular anger. Wikipedia – Kristallnacht

Scale and Course of the Pogroms

During the two nights, more than 1,400 synagogues and prayer houses and approximately 7,500 businesses were destroyed or damaged. At least 91 Jews were killed directly during the attacks, though historians point to further indirect victims as evidenced by the spike in suicides in November 1938. Police received explicit orders not to intervene; fire brigades were to let synagogues burn and act only if fire threatened "Aryan" property. SA paramilitary units, Hitler Youth and ordinary Germans destroyed shops, homes and cemeteries. Tonnes of broken glass from shattered shop windows were left in the streets – giving the event its name. Jewish Museum Berlin – November 9, 1938

Mass Arrests and Concentration Camps

In the following days, the Gestapo arrested approximately 30,000 Jewish men aged 16 to 60 and transported them to the concentration camps at Dachau, Buchenwald and Sachsenhausen, which had to be expanded to accommodate the sudden influx. Most of the detainees were released after a few weeks or months – on condition that they leave Germany and abandon their property. Jewish Museum Berlin – November 9, 1938

The Height of Nazi Cynicism: A Fine for One's Own Destruction

The peak of cynicism came at a meeting chaired by Hermann Göring on November 12: Germany's Jews were ordered to pay a collective fine of one billion Reichsmarks for damage inflicted on their own property. Their insurance payouts for the destroyed assets were simultaneously confiscated, and they were forced to clean up the broken glass from the streets themselves. Kristallnacht was thus deliberately used to accelerate forced "Aryanisation" – the state-organised expropriation of Jewish property – and to complete the exclusion of Jews from economic life. A Turning Point on the Road to the Holocaust

Kristallnacht represented a dramatic escalation of Nazi anti-Jewish policy. The regime moved from the racial discrimination enshrined in the 1935 Nuremberg Laws to open terror and mass violence. Historians regard the November pogrom as both a symbolic and practical watershed: it marked the transition to the complete exclusion of Jews from society and foreshadowed their physical annihilation. The events accelerated the exodus of Jews from Germany – emigration after Kristallnacht took on the character of a panicked flight. The pogrom also took place in the newly annexed Sudetenland, where the Czech borderlands had been occupied just weeks before. History.com – Kristallnacht
