29. FEBRUARY 1933 – Banning of Unions and Opposition

Over the course of less than five months – from late February to July 14, 1933 – the Nazi regime systematically destroyed all organized opposition to its power. This was a process without precedent in the history of democratic nations for its speed and thoroughness: in five months, what other dictatorships had taken years to build was eliminated.

May 2, 1933: The End of Free Trade Unions

On May 2, 1933, at the same hour throughout Germany, armed units of the SA and SS occupied union buildings, confiscated their assets, arrested leaders, and emptied the treasuries. The free German trade unions, which had organized over five million members and formed the backbone of workers' political culture since the Weimar Republic, ceased to exist overnight. They were replaced by the German Labor Front (Deutsche Arbeitsfront – DAF), controlled by the Nazi Party. The DAF was not a trade union in any sense – it did not represent workers against employers, did not organize strikes (which were banned), but served as a tool for ideological indoctrination and control of the workforce. Workers lost the right to collective bargaining and to free association in labor matters.

Banning of Political Parties

Concurrent with the destruction of unions was the dissolution of political parties. The KPD (Communists) was effectively banned in February 1933 after the Reichstag Fire. The SPD (Social Democrats) was banned on June 22, 1933, after its leadership emigrated; remaining functionaries in Germany were arrested or went into hiding. The other parties – the Center, Bavarian People's Party, State Party, German People's Party, and finally Hugenberg's German National People's Party – were "voluntarily" dissolved one after another under pressure and threats in June and July 1933. A law of July 14, 1933, then declared the NSDAP the only legal political party in Germany and made the establishment of any other party a criminal offense.

Unity Without Freedom

The result was a political vacuum filled by the monolithic structure of the NSDAP. Germans could still vote – but only from a single slate. They could be members of organizations – but only those under party control. They could work – but without the right to strike or organize independently. The concept of freedom was reduced to freedom to agree with what the party decided.
