March 15, 1939 – Occupation of Bohemia and Moravia

On March 15, 1939, German troops crossed the borders of the remaining Czech lands without a declaration of war. By the morning, Wehrmacht columns had entered Prague. Adolf Hitler arrived at Prague Castle the same evening and proclaimed the establishment of the Protectorate of Bohemia and Moravia. The existence of the Second Republic – already stripped of its borderlands by the Munich Agreement just six months earlier – came to a definitive end. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_occupation_of_Czechoslovakia [Wikipedia – German occupation of Czechoslovakia]

The Night of the Occupation

The occupation began in the early hours of March 15. President Emil Hácha had been summoned to Berlin the previous night, where Hitler and Göring subjected him to hours of intense pressure – threatening the immediate bombing of Prague. Hácha suffered a mild heart attack during the meeting and was reportedly revived by Hitler's personal physician. Under duress, he signed a document placing the fate of the Czech people in Hitler's hands. German units then moved in unresisted, as the Czechoslovak army had received orders not to fight. https://www.moderni-dejiny.cz/clanek/obsazeni-cech-a-moravy-15-brezna-1939/ [Moderní-Dějiny.cz – Occupation of Bohemia and Moravia]

End of Sovereignty – The Protectorate Proclaimed

The decree establishing the Protectorate of Bohemia and Moravia was issued on March 16, 1939. Czechoslovakia ceased to exist as a state. Parliament was dissolved, the government stripped of real authority, and the office of president reduced to a figurehead role. Konstantin von Neurath was appointed Reich Protector, holding effective executive power. Slovakia, formally declared independent the day before under pressure from Berlin, became a Nazi puppet state. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protectorate_of_Bohemia_and_Moravia [Wikipedia – Protectorate of Bohemia and Moravia]

Abolition of Civil Liberties

With the occupation, all civil rights gained in 1918 were swept away overnight. Freedom of speech, the press, assembly and association were abolished. Political parties – with the exception of those collaborating with the regime – were banned. The Gestapo established its headquarters in Prague and immediately began arresting politicians, journalists, academics and other public figures. The first transports of Czech citizens to concentration camps followed within weeks. https://www.holocaust.cz/dejiny/soa/protektorat-cechy-a-morava/ [Holocaust.cz – Protectorate of Bohemia and Moravia]

Persecution and Terror

The Nazi occupation brought systematic persecution of Jews, Roma and Czech political opponents. Jewish citizens were subjected to the same racial laws already in force in Germany. Universities were closed following student protests on November 17, 1939 – a date now commemorated as International Students' Day. Nine student leaders were executed without trial and over 1,200 students were sent to concentration camps. Czech culture, language and national identity became targets of deliberate suppression. https://www.dejepis.com/ucebnice/protektorat-cechy-a-morava/ [Dejepis.com – Protectorate of Bohemia and Moravia]

International Reaction and Historical Legacy

The occupation of the remaining Czech lands – territories with no significant German-speaking population – finally shattered the illusion that Hitler's demands were limited to ethnic Germans. For Britain and France, it exposed the failure of the appeasement policy pursued at Munich. Within weeks, Britain issued security guarantees to Poland. March 15 thus marked not only the end of Czech independence but also a decisive turning point on the road to the Second World War. In Czech historical memory, the date remains one of the most painful symbols of lost freedom and national humiliation. https://ct24.ceskatelevize.cz/clanek/domaci/15-brezen-1939-den-kdy-zanikla-ceskoslovenska-republika-75492 [ČT24 – March 15, 1939]
