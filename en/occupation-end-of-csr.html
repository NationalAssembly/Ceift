15. MARCH 1939 – Bohemia and Moravia occupation

In the early morning hours of March 15, 1939, columns of German troops crossed the borders of dismembered Czechoslovakia. Tanks and armored vehicles reached Prague. No resistance came – President Emil Hácha capitulated the previous night in Berlin after being subjected to brutal psychological pressure and threats of aerial bombardment of Prague. Hitler rode triumphantly through Prague and from Prague Castle announced the creation of the Protectorate of Bohemia and Moravia. It was a direct violation of the Munich Agreement, which had guaranteed the integrity of the remaining Czechoslovakia.

The End of the Rule of Law

With the arrival of the Germans, the rights guaranteed by the constitution of 1920 ceased to apply. Parliament was effectively dissolved, political parties banned (except the collaborationist National Solidarity), press freedom abolished, and censorship handed over to German authorities. The Gestapo began arrests on the very first day. Among the first were Communists, Social Democrats, active anti-Fascists, Jews, and those who had managed to speak publicly against Germany. Everything that had been built over twenty years as a democratic legal order was rolled up within hours like an unnecessary piece of paper.

Life Under the Protectorate

For the average inhabitant of Bohemia and Moravia, March 15 meant the beginning of existence under conditions without basic freedoms. Newspapers printed what the Reich Protector allowed. School curricula were rewritten in the spirit of Nazi ideology. Public assembly without permission was forbidden. Raids and document checks became a regular part of daily life. Arrests, interrogations by the Gestapo, and deportation to concentration camps ceased to be exceptional occurrences and became a real threat to anyone who resisted or merely aroused suspicion.

Loss of Freedom in Numbers

Over 80,000 Jews were deported from or forced to emigrate from Bohemia and Moravia, the vast majority of whom perished in Nazi extermination camps. Thousands of political prisoners passed through prisons and concentration camps. Academic freedom ended with the closure of Czech universities in November 1939. The entire intellectual and cultural stratum of society either emigrated, collaborated, or waited silently for the end. The freedom that the republic had achieved in 1918–1920 was completely erased in 1939.
