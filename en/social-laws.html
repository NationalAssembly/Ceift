DECEMBER 19, 1918 – SOCIAL LAWS OF THE FIRST REPUBLIC

Less than two months after declaring independence, the Revolutionary National Assembly faced the question of what the republic actually promised its workers. Political freedom – elections, a constitution, the end of Habsburg censorship – was not in itself enough for the factory worker who emerged from a twelve-hour shift. On December 19, 1918, therefore, the National Assembly passed three fundamental laws that together formed the foundation of the welfare state in the new republic: the Law on the Eight-Hour Workday (No. 91/1918 Coll.), the Law on Unemployment Support, and the Law on Workers' Sickness Insurance. Along with preparations for land reform, whose key expropriation law came in April 1919, this represented the most comprehensive social reconstruction in modern Czech history.

The Eight-Hour Workday: End of Long Shifts

Law No. 91/1918 Coll. stipulated that working hours could not "in principle exceed eight hours in twenty-four hours or a maximum of forty-eight hours per week." Beyond this, it established mandatory breaks, guarantees for night work and overtime, a ban on excessive work for minors, and an uninterrupted weekly rest of at least 32 hours – typically on Sunday. The law remained in effect without fundamental changes until 1965 and was one of the longest-lasting labor law provisions of the First Republic. Still in the early 18th century, workers labored up to 16 hours a day; the law of December 1918 transformed this standard into a norm protected by state power. Freedom after work – time for family, education, and political life – ceased to be the privilege of the wealthy.

Social Insurance: Protection Against Illness and Unemployment

The Law on Workers' Sickness Insurance introduced compulsory contributory insurance, which gave workers the right to sickness benefits in case of work incapacity. This was a fundamental shift: in the Habsburg Monarchy, social protection depended on the employer's will; the new law transformed it into a legal right. The Law on Unemployment Support provided the first systematic income guarantee for those who lost their jobs. Neither law provided complete coverage – benefits were low and conditions strict – but symbolically and practically, this was a breakthrough. For the first time, the state acknowledged that economic vulnerability of workers was not a private matter but a public concern requiring collective care.

Land Reform: Land for the Landless

The Land Reform Law, whose key expropriation provision – the Expropriation Law No. 215/1919 Coll. – took effect on April 16, 1919, was one of the most ambitious redistributive measures in the history of Czech lands. Large estates exceeding 150 hectares of agricultural or 250 hectares of total land were subject to state expropriation with compensation. The land was then distributed to landless peasants, smallholders, cottagers, and also legionnaires. The reform affected over four million hectares and primarily targeted aristocratic estates – the Schwarzenbergs, Liechtensteins, and Habsburg relatives. The anti-Habsburg and anti-aristocratic nature of the reform was deliberate: it represented a symbolic break with an era in which land and thus economic freedom were concentrated in the hands of a narrow hereditary class. For hundreds of thousands of rural landless peasants, the promise of land meant the first real step toward economic independence.

Social Freedom as a Condition of Political Freedom

December 1918 and spring 1919 showed that the republic's founders understood freedom more broadly than merely the freedom to vote or freedom of the press. Without protection in illness, without safeguards against dismissal, and without access to land, political rights remained an empty shell for much of the population. The social legislation of the republic's first months, therefore, is not merely a technical history of labor law – it is part of the same story of expanding freedom that began on October 28, 1918, on the streets of Prague.
