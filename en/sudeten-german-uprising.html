12. SEPTEMBER 1938 – Sudeten German Uprising

The night of September 12-13, 1938, was recorded in history as the moment when the crisis ceased to be merely diplomatic and became armed. Following Hitler's aggressive speech at the NSDAP congress in Nuremberg on September 12, a coordinated armed uprising of Sudeten German groups broke out in Czechoslovakia's border areas under the command of officials of Henlein's SdP and the affiliated Freikorps. Police stations, state offices, and Czech border residents were attacked. Over the course of a single night, several dozen people lost their lives.

Background: From Elections to Violence

The uprising did not come out of nowhere. The escalation had been building for months. In the spring of 1938, Henlein formulated the so-called Carlsbad demands, which in fact could not be accepted within a democratic state—they included autonomy of the border areas with their own administration under de facto German protectorate. Prague refused, negotiations repeatedly failed, and Henlein received instructions directly from Berlin. The situation on the borders gradually radicalized: reports came of paramilitary drills, weapons smuggling, and organized agitation. The August mission of British Lord Runciman, intended to mediate a compromise, ended in failure.

Martial Law and Loss of Freedom

The government responded by declaring martial law in the affected areas and deploying the army and police. Freedom of movement, assembly, and press in the border regions was drastically restricted. Thousands of people had to flee violence—among them Czech officials, teachers, but also democratically-minded Sudeten Germans who rejected Henlein's policies. They paid the highest price: they lost their homes and found themselves in a republic that accepted them but where their situation was desperate.

Prelude to Munich

The uprising of September 12, 1938, fulfilled its strategic purpose from Berlin's perspective: it created the impression of an uncontrollable crisis that "required" German intervention, and forced British and French leaders to accelerate their search for an agreement with Hitler. The result was Munich. Freedom, which had been built step by step over twenty years—elections, constitution, minority rights, press freedom—began to crumble to the rhythm of international diplomacy in which Czechoslovakia had no voice.
