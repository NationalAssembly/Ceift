15. SEPTEMBER 1935 – Nuremberg Laws

On September 15, 1935, the Reichstag, convened extraordinarily for the Nazi Party Congress in Nuremberg, passed two laws that encoded racial discrimination into German law. The Law on Reich Citizenship and the Law for the Protection of German Blood and German Honor – collectively known as the Nuremberg Laws – redefined who was a German and systematically stripped Jewish inhabitants of the basic rights that still remained to them.

The Content and Scope of the Laws

The Law on Reich Citizenship divided the inhabitants of Germany into two categories: Reich citizens (Reichsbürger), who held full political rights, and state subjects (Staatsangehörige) without political rights. Jews fell into the second category. They lost voting rights, could not hold public office, be civil servants, or serve in the military. The Law for the Protection of Blood forbade marriages and extramarital relations between Jews and "Aryans" under threat of criminal prosecution. Implementing ordinances then defined various degrees of "hybrids" (Mischlinge) and extended discrimination across the entire spectrum of human relations.

Loss of Fundamental Rights

For hundreds of thousands of German Jews, many of whom considered Germany their homeland, loved German culture and language, and had fought for the German flag in World War I, the Nuremberg Laws were a traumatic denial of belonging to the national community. They lost equality before the law, personal freedom, freedom of movement, choice of profession, and protection of property. What the Weimar Constitution had guaranteed to all citizens without distinction was now made racially conditional and simply taken from part of the population. The Nuremberg Laws were thus not merely discrimination – they were the legislative expression of the principle that freedom and rights are the privilege of race, not human dignity.

A Model for Others

The Nuremberg Laws were carefully studied in other countries where racist and antisemitic movements sought legislative models. Research by historian James Whitman showed that their creators also studied American racial segregation laws – and in some respects found American measures too radical and instead chose more moderate formulations. This detail illustrates how racism in the interwar period was an international phenomenon and the extent to which Nazi racial legislation drew from the international context of the time.
