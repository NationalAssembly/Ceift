29. FEBRUARY 1920 – Final Constitution

As a result of more than a year of legislative effort, the definitive Constitution of the Czechoslovak Republic was adopted on February 29, 1920, by the Constituent National Assembly. This document, containing 134 paragraphs, replaced the provisional constitutional arrangement of November 1918 and gave the republic a solid democratic foundation, which remained in effect – with various changes – until 1948. The constitution established Czechoslovakia as a democratic parliamentary state with an elected bicameral parliament, a responsible government, and an independent judiciary.

Rovnost jako ústavní princip

One of the most important features of the constitution was the entrenchment of the principle of equality of all citizens before the law regardless of gender, nationality, religious belief, or social origin. Sections devoted to fundamental rights and freedoms guaranteed freedom of speech, press, assembly, association, and religion. For women, this was the culmination of a process that began with the provisional constitution in November 1918: constitutional equality was now not just a proclamation but a norm of the highest legal force. Women could vote, run for office, own property, and enter into contracts on exactly the same terms as men – at a time when in many European countries these rights were still fighting for recognition.

Ochrana menšin jako záruka svobody

The constitution also contained extensive provisions for the protection of national minorities. Germans, Hungarians, Poles, Rusyns, and Jews had constitutionally guaranteed rights to education in their own language, their own cultural institutions, and political representation. This measure was partly imposed by international treaties from the Paris Peace Conference, but it was a commitment that Czechoslovakia – at least on paper – embraced as part of its democratic ethos. In practice, minority rights were implemented with varying degrees of success, but their constitutional guarantee was exceptional in Europe at the time.

Ústava jako protiváha habsburské zkušenosti

The constitution's authors were conscious in responding to experience with Habsburg centralism. Therefore, the constitution prohibited censorship (Article 117), guaranteed the inviolability of the home and postal secrecy, restricted conditions for arrest, and established administrative courts as insurance against arbitrary government action. It was a direct response to the methods by which the Habsburg state and its military courts treated citizens during the war. Thus the document was not only a legal text but also a statement about what the republic did not want to be.
