Peak of the Economic Crisis – February 15, 1933

On February 15, 1933, the Great Depression reached its deepest point in Czechoslovakia. Industrial output fell to approximately 60% of its 1929 level and the number of unemployed exceeded one million. The republic had entered its second decade optimistically – the Wall Street Crash of 1929 initially caused little alarm. It was only from 1930 that the crisis hit industry in full, and by 1932 and 1933 the situation had become critical. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Depression [Wikipedia – Great Depression]

Scope and Impact of the Crisis

The crisis struck agriculture first, a sector employing 60% of the population. It hit smallholders hardest, with mounting debt and the ever-present threat of foreclosure – particularly in the east of the country. The decline in industrial production in 1933 reached nearly 40% compared to pre-crisis levels, largely due to the export-oriented nature of the Czechoslovak economy. https://www.dejepis.com/ucebnice/hospodarska-krize-v-ceskoslovensku/ [Dejepis.com – The Economic Crisis in Czechoslovakia]

The regions hit hardest were Slovakia, Subcarpathian Ruthenia and the Bohemian borderlands. Emigration rose to 10,000–15,000 people per year. In 1929 Czechoslovakia had just 42,000 unemployed – within four years that figure had risen nearly twentyfold. https://temata.rozhlas.cz/cesi-v-ohrozeni-takhle-vysilal-rozhlas-v-dobach-krize-8176791/1 [Czech Radio – The Great Depression in the Early 1930s]

Social Measures and Food Vouchers

The government tried to address the crisis through state loans and organised social relief schemes. Trade-union-organised workers received unemployment benefit funded partly by the state. For unorganised workers, the state ran soup kitchens and distributed food vouchers nicknamed "žebračenky" (beggar's tickets). Cuts to public spending and reductions in civil-service wages steadily eroded confidence in the democratic system. https://edu.ceskatelevize.cz/video/14470-hospodarska-krize-a-jeji-spolecenske-souvislosti [ČT edu – The Economic Crisis and Its Social Consequences]

Political Consequences – Rise of Extremism

Over a million unemployed people became fertile ground for extremist movements. The crisis severely weakened the social and economic freedom of citizens – hundreds of thousands lost their jobs, their income and any real ability to shape their own lives. The economic slump radicalised the German-speaking population of the borderlands, the area worst affected by the crisis. Skilled agitators convinced unemployed young people that the "Czech" government in Prague was to blame for their misery – a message made all the more persuasive by comparison with Germany next door, where Hitler had come to power in 1933 and the economy appeared to be recovering. https://www.seznamzpravy.cz/clanek/magazin-historie-velka-hospodarska-krize-ceskoslovensko-284759 [Seznam Zprávy – Unemployment, Hunger, Suicides: How the 1930s Crisis Crushed Czechoslovakia]

The Great Depression and Hitler's rise to power in Germany in 1933 dramatically deepened the problem of the Sudeten German minority in Czechoslovakia. Konrad Henlein's Sudeten German Party won more than 1.2 million votes in the 1935 parliamentary elections, becoming the strongest party in the country. Czechoslovakia's economy never fully recovered from the crisis before the outbreak of the Second World War. https://ct24.ceskatelevize.cz/ekonomika/2573557-neni-krize-jako-krize-velka-deprese-30-let-prinesla-masovou-chudobu-a-migraci-lide [ČT24 – Not All Crises Are Equal]
