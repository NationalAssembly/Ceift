NOVEMBER 12, 1918 – INTRODUCTION OF THE 8-HOUR WORKDAY IN GERMANY

One day after the abdication of Emperor Wilhelm II, and in the same fevered atmosphere in which the Weimar Republic was being born, German industrialists and trade union leaders reached an agreement that transformed the daily lives of millions of workers. On November 12, 1918, an eight-hour maximum workday was formally established in Germany – the so-called Stinnes-Legien Agreement, named after its principal negotiators, industrialist Hugo Stinnes and trade union leader Carl Legien. It was the result of behind-the-scenes negotiations conducted in the final days of the war, and its outcome transformed the relationship between labor and capital in Germany in a way that would have seemed unthinkable just a year earlier.

Why the Industrialists Capitulated

The agreement was largely born of fear. The November Revolution, which brought down the empire, carried the threat of Bolshevization in the Russian manner, where the proletariat had physically expropriated factory owners a year before. German industrial magnates calculated that it was cheaper to concede to key worker demands than to risk nationalization of their enterprises by revolutionary soviets. On the other hand, trade unionists led by Carl Legien saw in the rapidly changing situation an opportunity to realize goals that unions had sought from lawmakers for decades. The result was a compromise: workers received an eight-hour workday with full maintenance of existing wages, recognition of unions as legitimate partners in collective bargaining, and the establishment of works councils. Industrialists retained private ownership of the means of production and avoided nationalization.

What It Meant for German Workers

Before 1918, German factory workers typically worked ten to twelve hours a day, sometimes more in certain industries. Long shifts were the norm, unquestioned – if you wanted work, you accepted the conditions. Shortening the workday to eight hours was not merely a matter of clock time: it meant the first real free time in the lives of industrial workers, time for family, education, civic life, and politics. The freedom won in the factory opened doors to the public sphere as well. In this sense, the Stinnes-Legien Agreement was more than a labor law reform – it was an element of the broader democratization occurring in German society after the fall of the empire on multiple fronts simultaneously.

Short-Term Achievement, Long-Term Tension

The agreement lasted in practice only until the early 1920s. Once industrialists had stabilized and the revolutionary wave receded, they began to undermine the eight-hour law through parliamentary means. In 1923, during hyperinflation and economic crisis, the government extended working hours to ten. The ZAG organization, which the agreement had created, dissolved that same year. Yet the Stinnes-Legien Agreement remained in the collective memory of the German labor movement as a symbol: proof that social rights could be won without revolution, and also a warning of how easily such gains could be lost if not backed by firm legal protections.
