18. JANUARY 1920 – Works Councils

On January 18, 1920, the Weimar National Assembly passed the Works Council Act (Betriebsrätegesetz). This legislation enshrined mandatory representation of employees in enterprises above a certain size and became one of the most important social results of the early Weimar Republic. The law was the result of a compromise between the demands of the labor movement—which wanted complete socialization and worker control over production—and the efforts of the government and industrialists to preserve the capitalist system in its fundamentals. The result was less radical than revolutionaries demanded, but nevertheless unprecedented in the German context.

What the Law Guaranteed

The law granted workers and employees the right to elect works councils, which had the right to co-decide on working conditions, working hours, dismissals, and social matters in enterprises. It was a direct democratization of the workplace: where previously only the employer decided, there now had to be negotiation and deliberation. The passage of the law was not without dramatic background: groups of protesting workers surrounded the parliament building and demanded more radical measures. Police dispersed the protest and more than forty people died—a tragedy that showed how tense the atmosphere was in the early Weimar years.

The Law in the Context of Freedom

From the perspective of the history of freedom, the works council law is important because it extended protection of workers into the economic and everyday sphere, where previous legal order left workers practically without protection. After decades in which employers had almost unlimited power over working conditions, the law brought a tool of legal defense directly to the workplace. It was part of a broader effort by the Weimar Republic to achieve social peace and realize social rights enshrined in the constitution. In this sense, it was one of the most tangible results of the November 1918 revolution for the everyday life of ordinary Germans—a freedom to negotiate, not just to suffer.

Long-term Impact

The 1920 law was abolished by the Nazis in 1933 along with the unions and works councils as such. Nevertheless, it left a lasting imprint: the system of worker co-determination (Mitbestimmung), which was restored and expanded after 1945 in the Federal Republic, draws directly from the ideas and experiences of Weimar legislation. The Weimar works council is thus one of the few positive legacies of the republic that survived the Nazi intermezzo and entered modern German social market economy.
