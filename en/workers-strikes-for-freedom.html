28. JANUARY 1918 – Workers' Strikes for Freedom

From January 28, 1918, a wave of massive strikes swept Germany, surpassing in scale and political force everything that had happened in the empire during the war. By contemporary estimates, more than a million workers struck in Berlin, the Ruhr, Hamburg, Saxony, Kiel, and other industrial centers. It was not a spontaneous uprising from hunger, although food shortages played a role – it was a coordinated political protest with specific demands, organized by leaders connected to the Independent Social Democrats (USPD) and revolutionary trade union leaders.

Požadavky: mír a svoboda

The central slogans of the strike were an immediate end to the war without annexations or reparations, improvement of catastrophic food supplies, and – crucially for our context of freedom – the restoration of democratic rights that had been systematically curtailed since July 1914. Striking workers demanded freedom of speech and press, the right to political organization, and democratization of the Prussian three-class voting system, which favored the propertied classes. These demands show that after three and a half years of war dictatorship, the working class understood the direct connection between war, authoritarian state control, and the loss of basic civic rights. The struggle for peace and the struggle for freedom were one and the same struggle.

Tvrdá represe a porážka stávky

The response of the government and military leadership was harsh. Troops were sent to the strike areas, martial law was declared in Berlin, hundreds of union leaders and political organizers were imprisoned, many workers were forcibly conscripted to the front as punishment for participating in the protest. The strike was crushed within about ten days. In terms of immediate results, it went home empty-handed – neither peace nor democratization was achieved. Nevertheless, the movement proved that war dictatorship did not have the unconditional consent of those most affected by it. A million strikers were too loud a signal for the government to ignore as a marginal phenomenon.

Předzvěst revoluce

The January 1918 strike would later prove to be a dress rehearsal for the revolutionary movement that would topple the monarchy less than a year later. The networks of organizers, experience with coordinating protest, and frustration from the strike's suppression led to the radicalization of part of the labor movement. When the end of war and revolution came in November 1918, many of those who had participated in the January strike were among those occupying factories and establishing councils of workers and soldiers. The 1918 strike thus lost its immediate struggle for freedom, but its defeat contributed to the timing and nature of the fall of Imperial Germany.
