Workers' Mass Strikes for Freedom – From January 1918

From January 28, 1918, Germany was swept by mass strikes that became the largest anti-war protest of the First World War. In Berlin alone, over 400,000 workers downed tools; across at least 69 German cities, more than one million industrial workers joined the strike. It was the third major political mass strike in Germany since 1916, and a direct forerunner of the November Revolution. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_January_strikes_of_1918 [Wikipedia – German January strikes of 1918]

Background and Causes

The strike was organised by the Independent Social Democratic Party of Germany (USPD) and the so-called Revolutionary Shop Stewards – workplace representatives who rejected the SPD's wartime truce policy. An important role was also played by the inspiration of the Russian October Revolution of 1917 and the German-Russian armistice, which raised hopes for peace negotiations. The strike followed on from the simultaneous January strike movement in Austria-Hungary, in which over 700,000 workers had taken part. https://www.historisches-lexikon-bayerns.de/Lexikon/Januarstreik_(1918) [Historisches Lexikon Bayerns – Januarstreik 1918]

The Strikers' Demands

Berlin strikers elected 414 factory delegates who constituted themselves as the Greater Berlin Workers' Council. It formulated the key demands: an immediate peace settlement without annexations or reparations, improved food supplies, release of political prisoners, abolition of censorship and military control over industry, and democratisation of Germany – in particular the introduction of universal equal suffrage in Prussia. The strike was thus a direct protest against the military dictatorship of Hindenburg and Ludendorff and a call for the restoration of political freedoms drastically curtailed since 1914. https://www.dhm.de/lemo/kapitel/erster-weltkrieg/innenpolitik/januarstreik-1918 [Deutsches Historisches Museum – Januarstreik 1918]

Suppression of the Strike

The government and military responded harshly. On January 31, 1918, a heightened state of siege was declared over Berlin, as the government and army feared an armed uprising in the capital. Demonstrations and rallies were violently dispersed, with casualties. Striking enterprises were placed under military control. Strikers were given a deadline of February 4: return to work or face arrest or conscription to the front. On February 3, the Revolutionary Shop Stewards called off the strike in Berlin. https://www.dhm.de/lemo/kapitel/erster-weltkrieg/innenpolitik/januarstreik-1918 [Deutsches Historisches Museum – Januarstreik 1918]

Legacy and Historical Significance

Although the strike ended in defeat, it exposed the deep discontent with the wartime regime. It was only in October 1918, when military collapse could no longer be concealed, that a parliamentary government with Social Democratic participation was formed – one of the strikers' key demands fulfilled nearly a year later. By that point, however, the mood among the working class had radicalised to such an extent that reform measures could no longer contain it. The January strike is regarded as a direct precursor of the November Revolution of 1918 – many of the workers' and soldiers' councils that spontaneously emerged at that time were modelled on the Greater Berlin Workers' Council of January 1918. https://www.fes.de/news/januarstreik-1918 [Friedrich-Ebert-Stiftung – Der Januarstreik 1918]
