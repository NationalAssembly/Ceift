23. MARCH 1933 – Enabling Act

On March 23, 1933, one of the most mournful votes in the history of modern parliamentary democracy took place. The Reichstag – reduced to a fraction of its original composition by mass arrests of Communists and intimidation of Social Democrats, meeting in the Berlin Kroll Opera surrounded by armed members of the SS and SA – voted on the Enabling Act (Ermächtigungsgesetz). The result: 441 in favor, 94 against. Those 94 were Social Democrats; they alone voted no.

What the Law Allowed

The Enabling Act transferred legislative power from the parliament to Hitler's government for a period of four years – without any parliamentary oversight and even with the ability to enact laws that would contradict the constitution. Hitler thus legally received the power to issue decrees, conclude international treaties, and change the state system without parliamentary approval. What seemed on February 28 to be temporary emergency measures was now given legal, constitutionally enshrined form as dictatorship. The Reichstag became a backdrop, its sessions theater approving decisions made elsewhere.

The Last Voice of Freedom: The SPD

SPD leader Otto Wels delivered a speech in the Kroll Opera in which he rejected the law on behalf of his party. He spoke calmly and with dignity, aware that the voice of 94 deputies would change nothing. His words – "You can persecute us, but you cannot defeat us, cannot destroy our ideas" – became one of the most famous statements in German parliamentarism. Wels knew what awaited him: within weeks he was forced to emigrate and the SPD was banned. But the gesture of refusal remained recorded in the protocols as proof that not everyone surrendered.

The Final End of the Weimar Republic

March 23, 1933, is the date often marked as the real end of the Weimar Republic. All previous measures – emergency decrees, arrests, the Reichstag Fire – could theoretically have been reversed or revoked. After the Enabling Act, that path was closed. The freedoms enshrined in the constitution of 1919 ceased to have any real meaning.
