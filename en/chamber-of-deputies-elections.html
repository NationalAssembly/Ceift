18. APRIL 1920 – Chamber of Deputies Elections

On April 18, 1920, free elections to the parliament of their own state were held for the first time in Czech history. The elections to the Chamber of Deputies, whose results were supplemented a week later by Senate elections (April 25), were not merely an administrative act. They were confirmation that the republic was serious about what it had written into its constitution: that power comes from the people and is exercised by them through their representatives.

Free Elections After Centuries

The Austro-Hungarian monarchy had held elections to the Imperial Parliament or provincial diets, but these were historically conditional on property qualifications, national inequality, and government interference. Only in 1907 was universal male suffrage introduced in the Austrian half – but women and inhabitants of Hungary (including Slovaks) remained excluded. The 1920 elections were therefore historically exceptional: for the first time, all adult citizens of the republic could vote regardless of gender, property, or nationality. For the first time, women stepped to the polls, their right guaranteed by the constitution adopted just six weeks earlier.

Results and Political Plurality

The elections confirmed the diversity of the young republic's political scene. The strongest party was the Czechoslovak Social Democratic Workers' Party with over 25% of votes, closely followed by the Agrarians. National Democrats, Catholic People's Party, and various German parties that chose to participate in the democratic process maintained strong positions. Turnout exceeded 89%, showing that citizens took their new right to vote seriously. The elections created the first regular parliament of the CSR and closed the transitional period of the revolutionary National Assembly.

What the Elections Meant for Freedom

The election results were binding, and the government formed from them bore parliamentary responsibility. This was a fundamental difference from the past, when prime ministers were appointed by the monarch. Now the mandate flowed from the voters. For Czechs, Slovaks, Germans, and other inhabitants of the republic, this was tangible proof that the freedom to vote and be elected was not merely a slogan but the actual norm of the new state.
