30. JANUARY 1933 – Hitler as Chancellor

On the afternoon of January 30, 1933, eighty-eight-year-old President Paul von Hindenburg appointed Adolf Hitler as Reich Chancellor. This was a decision that changed the course of European history. Hitler did not come to power through a coup d'état or revolutionary street battles – he came through legal means, appointed to office under the prevailing Weimar Constitution. This is a fact that the Nazis themselves emphasized for propaganda purposes and which became one of the most traumatic aspects of the entire event: democracy opened the door to its own grave-digger.

Jak k tomu došlo

Hindenburg appointed Hitler after months of political crisis and repeated failures to form a functioning parliamentary government. The president's conservative advisors – Franz von Papen and Alfred Hugenberg – convinced Hindenburg that Hitler could be "tamed" by surrounding him with conservative ministers and thus limiting his scope. This calculation turned out to be one of the greatest political miscalculations of modern times. Hitler quickly rid his coalition government of conservative control and within less than a year became an absolute ruler.

Začátek konce Výmarské republiky

For historians of freedom, January 30, 1933, is the date from which we count backward to the destruction of German democracy. The freedoms that the Weimar Constitution guaranteed were not abolished by a single act on that day – but Hitler's appointment as chancellor set in motion a process that led to their systematic liquidation over the following months. The inhabitants of Berlin who watched the evening torch-lit procession of Nazis under the windows of the Reich Chancellery could not know what was coming. Some had suspicions; those who had carefully read Hitler's Mein Kampf could have certainty. But democratic Europe as a whole – and Germany itself – still refused to fully confront what the Nazi program actually meant.
