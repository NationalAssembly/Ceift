18. DECEMBER 1935 – Beneš as President

On December 18, 1935, the National Assembly elected Edvard Beneš as the second president of the Czechoslovak Republic. Beneš, who had served as foreign minister throughout the First Republic and was Masaryk's closest political collaborator, won by a relatively narrow margin over the Agrarian Party's candidate Bohumil Němec. The election took place shortly after Tomáš Garrigue Masaryk, in poor health, had abdicated – on December 14, 1935, after seventeen years in office.

Beneš jako nástupce Masaryka

Edvard Beneš was Masaryk's natural successor in the sense that he shared his foreign policy philosophy: Czechoslovakia should rely on a system of collective security, cooperation with France, the League of Nations, and adherence to the Versailles order. Beneš was an excellent diplomat but a less convincing leader in domestic politics. Where Masaryk ruled by moral authority, Beneš had to rely on party coalitions and behind-the-scenes negotiations. This weakness proved fatal when the republic faced an existential crisis.

Svoboda republiky v rukou diplomata

Beneš's election expressed both hope and fear. Hope that the republic would survive Nazi pressure through diplomacy, alliances, and international law. Fear, because the situation was deteriorating faster than diplomatic tools could respond to. Beneš believed in Europe as a system of rules and treaties – and Munich in 1938 definitively destroyed that belief. Nevertheless, his presidential mandate in the years 1935–1938 was a period in which the republic still formally and actually defended its freedom and democratic institutions functioned normally.
