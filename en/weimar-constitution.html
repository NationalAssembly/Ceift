11. AUGUST 1919 – Weimar Constitution

On August 11, 1919, Friedrich Ebert, as the first president of the Weimar Republic, signed the constitution that gave the new state a democratic order. The Weimar Constitution was among the most progressive fundamental laws of its time in Europe and in many respects was ahead of its era. Its authors—particularly constitutional scholar Hugo Preuss—consciously learned from the experiences of older democracies and from the mistakes committed by authoritarian monarchies. The result was a document that guaranteed Germany's inhabitants freedoms on paper that a year before they could only have dreamed of.

Fundamental Rights and Freedoms

The second part of the constitution, devoted to fundamental rights (Grundrechte), enshrined freedom of speech, press, assembly, association, and religion. It guaranteed equality of all citizens before the law regardless of gender, origin, or belief. It confirmed universal voting rights including for women and also contained modern social rights—the right to work, protection of motherhood, free education. For Germans who had experienced systematic suppression of basic rights during the war, this catalog of freedoms was not an abstraction—it was a direct response to concrete experience of lawlessness. The prohibition of censorship was explicitly enshrined in Article 118; the inviolability of the home, postal secrecy, and protection of personal freedom formed a solid pillar of the new legal order.

Strengths and Weaknesses of the Constitution

The constitution was an extraordinary document, but it carried within it the seeds of future trouble. The most critically commented feature was Article 48, which allowed the president, in case of threat to public order, to issue emergency decrees with the force of law without parliamentary approval. This article was created as insurance for exceptional situations—but it was used so many times that it became essentially a tool for bypassing parliamentary democracy. It was precisely this same mechanism that Paul von Hindenburg used in 1933 to open the path to power for the Nazis. The 1919 constitution thus carried within it a tragic paradox: it contained the most carefully thought-out guarantees of freedom that Germany had ever known, and at the same time a mechanism that could nullify these guarantees in a moment of crisis.

Historical Contribution and Legacy

Despite all its weaknesses, the Weimar Constitution is a milestone in the history of German freedom. For the first time, Germany became a state whose fundamental law was not the will of a ruler, but written and guaranteed citizens' rights. This precedent was so powerful that the authors of the Basic Law of the Federal Republic of Germany after 1949 consciously referenced the Weimar Constitution—and, learning from its mistakes, corrected its greatest weaknesses.
