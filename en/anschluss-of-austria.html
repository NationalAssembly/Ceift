12. MARCH 1938 – Anschluss of Austria

On March 12, 1938, German military columns crossed Austria's state border and occupied the country without resistance. The Anschluss – the union – was no surprise to those in the know: pressure from Berlin had been mounting since 1933 and culminated in an ultimatum that forced Austrian Chancellor Kurt Schuschnigg to resign. Austrian Nazi sympathizer Arthur Seyss-Inquart took his place and formally "requested" German troops. The Law on the Reunion of Austria with the German Reich was passed on March 13, and Österreich ceased to exist as an independent state.

The End of Austrian Statehood

For Austria, the Anschluss represented a complete loss of state sovereignty. The independent state that had emerged from the ruins of the Austro-Hungarian monarchy in 1918 disappeared overnight. Parliament was dissolved, political parties banned (except the NSDAP), press freedom abolished, and censorship transferred to German authorities. Vienna erupted in a wave of antisemitic violence – more spontaneous and in some ways more savage than what occurred in Germany itself. Viennese Jews were forced to clean streets, publicly humiliated, and immediately dismissed from their jobs. The mass exodus of Jews from Vienna began immediately after the Anschluss.

Hitler's Popularity and the Joy of the Crowds

It is historically uncomfortable but unavoidable that much of the Austrian population welcomed the Anschluss. Vienna received Hitler with jubilant crowds in Heldenplatz. A referendum held after the Anschluss showed over 99 percent in favor – a manipulated figure, but one revealing far less actual resistance than one might expect. The Anschluss was a power victory for Nazi Germany even without a single shot fired: it added industry, the National Bank's gold, and over six million new "Reich Germans." Hitler's popularity in both Germany and annexed Austria skyrocketed, and the resolve of democratic powers to confront Nazi expansion significantly weakened.
